## The Documentation Problem in EMS

EMS documentation is uniquely difficult for reasons that have nothing to do with effort or competence. The constraints are structural:

- **Cognitive load during the call:** You're managing a patient, not taking notes
- **Time pressure post-call:** The next run may be incoming before you've finished the report
- **Legal exposure:** PCR documentation is a legal record that follows the patient through the health system
- **Clinical handoff:** ED staff use your documentation to inform treatment decisions
- **QA/QI:** Your agency reviews PCRs for protocol adherence and outcome tracking

The industry solution for decades has been to do the best you can and accept that some details will fall through. Granola changes the calculus.

## Being Present When It Matters Most

There's a clinical cost to divided attention during patient care. When you're thinking about how you're going to document an intervention, you're not fully processing the patient in front of you.

The best prehospital providers are completely in the moment — running through their differential, reading subtle signs (skin color, respiratory effort, affect), calibrating their interventions based on patient response. The documentation layer is a distraction they manage, not a support system that helps them work.

Granola's approach is to let you focus entirely on the patient during the call, then use the captured audio to build accurate documentation afterward. You don't have to hold details in working memory while managing an airway or running a 12-lead. The audio is there when you need it.

## Prehospital Assessment Documentation

A complete patient assessment generates a lot of information quickly. Chief complaint, onset, provocation, quality, radiation, severity, timing (OPQRST). Allergies, medications, pertinent medical history, last oral intake, events leading up (SAMPLE). Vitals at contact, during transport, at handoff. Physical exam findings. Mental status changes. Intervention responses.

That's a lot to reconstruct from memory forty-five minutes after the call, after you've had two more patients since then.

With Granola running, the verbal assessment — the questions you asked, the answers you got, the findings you called out to your partner — creates a retrievable record. When you sit down to write the PCR, you're working from audio documentation, not memory fragments.

The result is more complete, more accurate reports with better time stamps and better clinical detail.

## Handoff Quality and ED Communication

The verbal handoff from EMS to ED staff is one of the most critical communication events in prehospital care. It needs to convey:

- Patient demographics and chief complaint
- History of present illness
- Pertinent medical/surgical history
- Medications and allergies
- Assessment findings (mental status, vitals, exam)
- Interventions and patient response
- Current status and anticipated trajectory

Under time pressure, in a busy emergency department, with a sick patient on the stretcher, details get dropped. ED staff make treatment decisions based on incomplete information. Granola gives you a structured record you can reference while giving report — and gives the ED a documentation trail they can access if they need to reconstruct the prehospital timeline.

## Protocol Compliance Documentation

EMS agencies run on protocols. Every intervention — oxygen administration, IV access, medication administration, CPAP, 12-lead interpretation, spinal immobilization — has documentation requirements tied to protocol criteria. Getting those criteria documented correctly matters for QA, for billing, and for legal protection.

After a high-acuity run with multiple interventions happening in sequence, documenting the clinical justification for each one is genuinely difficult. You remember that you gave aspirin. You may not remember whether you documented the patient's lack of allergies and confirmed symptom onset before administration.

With Granola capturing the verbal components of the call, the clinical justifications that you verbalized during the run — "Patient denies aspirin allergy, onset less than six hours, I'm going to give aspirin 324 milligrams" — become retrievable documentation anchors.

## Multi-Crew and Multi-Agency Calls

Major incidents — MVAs with multiple patients, structure fires with casualties, cardiac arrest with extended resuscitation — involve multiple providers and multiple agencies. The documentation burden multiplies, and the communication complexity means that important handoffs happen verbally and then have to be reconstructed.

Granola captures the multi-party communication on complex calls, giving you a record of what information was communicated between providers, when interventions happened, and who made which decisions. For post-incident review and for legal protection, that record has value that's hard to overstate.

## Setting Up Granola for EMS Work

The practical setup for prehospital use centers on one question: how do you run audio capture in a moving ambulance with multiple people talking and radio traffic in the background?

Granola's noise handling is solid in most environments. For EMS work, a discreet earpiece or portable Bluetooth mic helps distinguish patient and crew verbal from radio traffic and ambient noise. The setup takes a few minutes before a shift and runs passively through the day.

Privacy considerations are real — patient audio is PHI. The workflow that works best for most providers is using Granola to capture assessment and handoff audio in the controlled environment of the unit, then deleting the raw audio after the PCR is completed and verified. The documentation output, not the raw audio, is what persists.
