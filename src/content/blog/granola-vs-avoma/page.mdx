## Transcription and AI Analysis

### Granola's Approach

Granola captures audio locally on your Mac, processes it with a fine-tuned transcription model, and then runs AI summarization that understands *context* — not just words. It knows the difference between a product roadmap discussion and a sales call, and adapts its summary structure accordingly.

The result is summaries that read like something a thoughtful human wrote: structured, relevant, and free of filler. You can customize templates per meeting type (1:1s, sprint reviews, client calls) so recurring meetings always produce consistent outputs.

### Avoma's Approach

Avoma is purpose-built for revenue organizations. Its AI analysis emphasizes talk-time ratios, topic detection (pricing discussions, competitor mentions, objections), and scoring calls against customizable rubrics. For sales managers reviewing rep performance, this is genuinely powerful.

However, outside of sales and customer success contexts, Avoma's analysis can feel heavy-handed. General team meetings don't benefit much from talk-ratio dashboards or deal-stage detection.

## Pricing Deep Dive

Granola's pricing starts around $18/month per user with a generous free tier for individuals. The cost scales gently with team size and doesn't gatekeep core features behind enterprise tiers.

Avoma's pricing starts at $49/month per user for the Plus plan, climbing to $129/month for the Business tier that includes advanced coaching and CRM automation. For a 10-person sales team, that's $490–$1,290/month versus roughly $180 for Granola.

If you're a sales organization with heavy CRM requirements, Avoma's per-seat cost might be justified. For everyone else, it's hard to defend the premium.

## Privacy and Security

Granola processes audio locally by default — your meeting content never leaves your machine unless you choose to sync. This is a significant advantage for professionals dealing with sensitive conversations: lawyers, therapists, financial advisors, and healthcare workers.

Avoma, as a cloud-first platform, sends all audio and transcripts to its servers for processing. It maintains SOC 2 Type II compliance, which is table stakes for enterprise, but local-first privacy isn't an option.
