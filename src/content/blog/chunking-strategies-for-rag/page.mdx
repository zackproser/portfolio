# A Practical Guide to Chunking Strategies for RAG

Chunking is the cheapest knob in your RAG pipeline and the one that breaks retrieval most often. You can swap embedding models, upgrade your vector database, and rewrite your prompts, and still get garbage answers because you split your documents wrong three steps earlier.

I spent two years building and fixing RAG systems at Pinecone. The single most common root cause I saw for "the bot gives bad answers" wasn't the model, the prompt, or the index. It was chunking. Either the chunks were too big and the relevant sentence got drowned out by noise, or they were too small and the answer got severed across a boundary. The fix almost never lived in the LLM. It lived in how the text got cut.

This is a practical tour of the three chunking strategies you'll reach for — fixed-size, semantic, and structural — with real code, the tradeoffs, and a decision table for picking one.

## Why chunking decides your retrieval quality

A chunk is the unit of retrieval. When a user asks a question, you embed their query, find the nearest chunks in vector space, and stuff those chunks into the model's context. The model can only answer from what you retrieved. So the chunk boundary is the boundary of what your system can know in a single shot.

That gives you two failure modes, and they pull in opposite directions:

- **Chunks too large.** The relevant sentence is in there, but it's buried among 900 characters of unrelated text. The embedding averages out to something mushy, the cosine similarity to the query drops, and your chunk loses to a more focused but less correct neighbor. Even when you do retrieve it, you've spent context budget on noise.
- **Chunks too small.** The answer spans a boundary. The definition is in chunk 4, the caveat that changes the answer is in chunk 5, and you only retrieved chunk 4. The model confidently tells the user something half-true.

Every strategy below is a different answer to one question: **where do you put the cuts so each chunk is self-contained enough to answer a question, but focused enough to win the similarity search?** If you want the full end-to-end pipeline this fits into, I walked through one in my [RAG pipeline tutorial](/blog/rag-pipeline-tutorial). This post zooms in on the splitting step.

## Fixed-size chunking

Fixed-size chunking splits text every N characters or tokens, usually with some overlap so you don't sever sentences at the seam. It's the default in nearly every framework, and for good reason: it's predictable, fast, and embarrassingly hard to beat on a lot of real corpora.

Here's the version most people should start with, a recursive splitter that tries to break on paragraph, then sentence, then word boundaries before it resorts to a hard character cut:

```python
from langchain_text_splitters import RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter

splitter = RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter(
    chunk_size=1000,
    chunk_overlap=150,
    # Try these separators in order; fall back to a hard cut only as a last resort.
    separators=["\n\n", "\n", ". ", " ", ""],
    length_function=len,
)

chunks = splitter.split_text(document_text)
print(f"{len(chunks)} chunks, avg {sum(len(c) for c in chunks) // len(chunks)} chars")
```

The `chunk_overlap` matters more than people expect. With zero overlap, a sentence that straddles a boundary gets cut in half and neither chunk carries the full thought. 100–200 characters of overlap (or roughly 10–20% of `chunk_size`) buys you cheap insurance against that. The cost is duplication: overlapping text means more vectors, more storage, and the same sentence can show up in two retrieved chunks.

**When fixed-size wins:**

- Your documents are homogeneous prose without strong structure: transcripts, support tickets, scraped article bodies.
- You want a baseline fast. Always build the fixed-size baseline first and measure it before you reach for anything fancier.
- You care about predictable cost. Fixed-size gives you a tight, knowable distribution of chunk sizes, which makes embedding spend and context budgeting easy to reason about.

**Where it hurts:**

- It's blind to meaning. A 1000-character window will happily cut through the middle of an argument because character 1000 happened to land there.
- It's blind to structure. It'll merge the end of one section and the start of the next into a single chunk that's about two different things.

For a huge fraction of production systems, a well-tuned recursive splitter with sane overlap is the right answer, and the more sophisticated strategies below earn their complexity only when fixed-size demonstrably falls short on your eval set.

## Semantic chunking

Semantic chunking puts the boundaries where the *meaning* shifts instead of where a character counter ticks over. The mechanism: embed each sentence, walk through the document, and start a new chunk whenever consecutive sentences become dissimilar enough in embedding space. A topic change shows up as a spike in embedding distance, and you cut there.

```python
import numpy as np
from openai import OpenAI

client = OpenAI()

def embed(texts):
    resp = client.embeddings.create(model="text-embedding-3-small", input=texts)
    return np.array([d.embedding for d in resp.data])

def semantic_chunks(sentences, percentile=90):
    if len(sentences) == 0:
        return []
    if len(sentences) == 1:
        return [sentences[0]]
    
    vectors = embed(sentences)
    # Cosine distance between each sentence and the next.
    distances = []
    for i in range(len(sentences) - 1):
        a, b = vectors[i], vectors[i + 1]
        cos_sim = a @ b / (np.linalg.norm(a) * np.linalg.norm(b))
        distances.append(1 - cos_sim)

    # Cut wherever the topic shift is in the top decile of jumps.
    threshold = np.percentile(distances, percentile)
    chunks, current = [], [sentences[0]]
    for i, dist in enumerate(distances):
        if dist >= threshold:
            chunks.append(" ".join(current))
            current = [sentences[i + 1]]
        else:
            current.append(sentences[i + 1])
    chunks.append(" ".join(current))
    return chunks
```

The `percentile` threshold is the dial. Lower it and you cut more aggressively (smaller, tighter chunks); raise it and you cut only at the sharpest topic changes (larger, more cohesive chunks). LangChain and LlamaIndex both ship semantic splitters that wrap this idea if you don't want to hand-roll it.

**When semantic chunking wins:**

- Documents with topic drift inside a single long flow: a research paper that moves from background to method to results with no headers, a long-form essay, a meeting transcript that wanders across agenda items.
- Cases where fixed-size keeps gluing unrelated ideas together and your eval scores say so.

**Where it hurts:**

- It costs an embedding call per sentence at index time. On a large corpus that's real money and real latency before you've embedded a single final chunk.
- Chunk sizes become unpredictable. One section might produce a 200-character chunk and the next a 2,500-character one, which complicates context budgeting and can blow past your embedding model's token limit if you're not careful.
- It's only as good as the sentence-similarity signal. On dense technical text where every sentence is "close" in embedding space, the topic-shift spikes get muddy and the cuts land in odd places.

Semantic chunking is the one people reach for first because it sounds the smartest. In practice it's a targeted fix for a specific problem, topic drift in unstructured prose, not a default. Reach for it when your eval set shows fixed-size is mixing topics, not before.

## Structural chunking

Structural chunking uses the document's own skeleton (headings, sections, list items, code blocks, table rows) as the boundaries. If your content has structure, this is usually the strongest strategy, because the author already did the hard work of grouping related ideas. You only have to respect it.

Markdown is the easy case. Split on headers and carry the header hierarchy into each chunk's metadata:

```python
from langchain_text_splitters import (
    MarkdownHeaderTextSplitter,
    RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter,
)

headers = [("#", "h1"), ("##", "h2"), ("###", "h3")]

md_splitter = MarkdownHeaderTextSplitter(headers_to_split_on=headers)
sections = md_splitter.split_text(markdown_doc)

# Sections preserve their heading context in metadata, e.g.
# {"h1": "Billing", "h2": "Refunds"} -> attach to the chunk.
# Then guard against any one section being too large for the embedder.
char_splitter = RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter(chunk_size=1000, chunk_overlap=150)
chunks = char_splitter.split_documents(sections)
```

The pattern that matters: **split on structure first, then fall back to fixed-size only to cap oversized sections.** A short section stays whole. A giant section gets sub-split, but every sub-chunk still carries `Billing > Refunds` in its metadata so retrieval and the LLM both know where it came from. That header breadcrumb is genuinely useful at query time. You can show it as a citation, filter on it, or feed it to the model as context about the chunk's origin.

This is also where ingestion quality pays off. Structural chunking only works if your documents *arrive* with clean structure, and most raw HTML doesn't. It's nav bars, cookie banners, and `<div>` soup. I run pages through <AffiliateLink product="firecrawl" campaign={campaign}>Firecrawl</AffiliateLink> to turn messy sites into clean markdown with the heading hierarchy intact, which means the header splitter above has something real to split on. Garbage structure in, garbage chunks out, so it's worth getting the markdown clean before you ever touch a splitter. I went deeper on that ingestion step in [using Firecrawl for RAG pipelines](/blog/firecrawl-for-rag-pipelines).

Code and structured formats deserve their own handling. Splitting source code every 1000 characters will sever a function in half; instead, split on language-aware boundaries so functions and classes stay intact:

```python
from langchain_text_splitters import RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter, Language

code_splitter = RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter.from_language(
    language=Language.PYTHON,
    chunk_size=1200,
    chunk_overlap=100,
)
code_chunks = code_splitter.split_text(source_file)
```

**When structural chunking wins:**

- Anything with real structure: documentation, markdown, HTML, wikis, legal contracts with numbered clauses, codebases, API references.
- When you want citations and metadata filtering. Structure gives you free, accurate breadcrumbs to attach to every chunk.

**Where it hurts:**

- It does nothing for structureless blobs. A wall-of-text transcript has no headers to lean on, so you fall back to fixed-size or semantic.
- Inconsistent structure is its own trap. If half your docs use `##` for sections and half use bold text, you need normalization before the splitter behaves.

## The decision: which one to use

There's no universally best strategy. There's a best one for *your* documents. Here's how I decide:

<Image src="https://zackproser.b-cdn.net/images/chunking-strategies-for-rag-three-ways.jpg" alt="Comparison diagram showing the same document chunked three ways: fixed-size at even intervals, semantic at topic-shift points, and structural along heading boundaries" width={1200} height={675} />

| Your documents | Start with | Add if eval scores say so |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Clean markdown / docs / wikis | Structural (header split) | Fixed-size cap on huge sections |
| Source code | Structural (language-aware) | — |
| Plain transcripts / tickets / scraped prose | Fixed-size (recursive + overlap) | Semantic if topics keep mixing |
| Long unstructured essays / papers | Fixed-size baseline | Semantic for topic-drift sections |
| Mixed corpus | Route by type | Structural where structure exists, fixed-size elsewhere |

Two rules that survive every project:

1. **Build the fixed-size baseline first and measure it.** It's a few lines of code and it's the bar everything else has to clear. Plenty of teams burn a week on semantic chunking that their eval set says performs the same as a recursive splitter with 15% overlap.
2. **Match the strategy to the structure your documents actually have, not the one you wish they had.** Structural chunking is the strongest tool when structure exists. Semantic is the targeted fix when it doesn't and topics drift. Fixed-size is the reliable default underneath both.

## The knobs that move retrieval more than the strategy

Picking a strategy is half the work. These details quietly decide more than the fixed-vs-semantic-vs-structural choice does:

- **Overlap.** 10–20% overlap protects boundary-straddling sentences for nearly free. Start at 150 characters on 1000-character chunks and tune from there.
- **Metadata on every chunk.** Attach source, section/heading path, and document ID. You'll use it for citations, for filtering ("only search the billing docs"), and for debugging why a bad chunk got retrieved. This is the single highest-return habit in RAG and almost nobody does it on the first pass.
- **Chunk size vs. your embedding model.** Every embedding model has a token ceiling. `text-embedding-3-large` is 8,191 tokens. A chunk that exceeds it gets silently truncated, and you embed half a thought without knowing it. Cap your chunks well under the limit.
- **Measure, don't vibe.** Chunking changes are exactly the kind of thing you should A/B against a real eval set, because intuition is unreliable here. I wrote a whole piece on [RAG evaluation](/blog/rag-evaluation). Set up the eval loop before you start tuning chunk sizes, or you're guessing.

## How to pick, concretely

If you want a default to start from today: run a `RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter` at 1000 characters with 150 of overlap, attach source and section metadata to every chunk, and measure it against your eval set. That baseline is right more often than it has any business being.

Then let the failures tell you where to go. Chunks mixing two topics in unstructured prose? Try semantic. Documents with real headings you're ignoring? Switch to structural and carry the breadcrumbs into metadata. Functions getting cut in half? Language-aware splitting. Each upgrade should earn its complexity with a measurable bump on your eval set, not because it sounds more sophisticated.

Chunking isn't the glamorous part of RAG. It's the part that decides whether the rest of your pipeline ever gets a chance to work. Spend an afternoon on it before you spend a week swapping models.
