Mike was at every party. Never quite at one.

Tall, broad-shouldered. Short brown hair. Black leather jacket over everything. Eyes red as cherry pie — not bloodshot, not tired. Red. The kind of red that doesn't make sense in a twenty-year-old but was just there, like someone had left a taillight on inside his skull.

His parents were psychologists. Both of them.

They came to visit campus freshman year. Saw me standing with Mike. Said: *It looks like you and Mike are becoming friends.*

Asked for my phone number. I gave it. Didn't want to — didn't have the language for refusal at nineteen. Didn't know myself well enough to know I wasn't choosing this.

Later I pulled back. Stopped returning calls. Stopped showing up. Mike told his parents.

They called me. Asked if Mike and I were having a falling out.

Two psychologists tracking their son's only campus friend from a thousand miles. Already-trained eyes watching the attachment come apart in real time. The call felt like a spotlight. I was already gone. I just didn't have the words for it yet.

At parties he stood on the outside of every group. Not trying to be cool. Not aloof. Just — outside. Chain-smoking. Eyes the color of brake lights. That smile he tried on like he was borrowing it from someone who'd been welcomed.

Nobody talked to him about it. We all just watched him try. And when he walked away: *bright but off.* That was the verdict. Delivered to no one. Spoken after.

I heard it. I was in those rooms. I said nothing.

---

Couples therapy. The ex was asked what stood out about me.

*Intense.*

The first word. The only one I remember.

  src="https://zackproser.b-cdn.net/images/intensity-and-i-therapy.webp"

She said it in front of the therapist. Intense. Like it was a diagnosis. Maybe it was.

I thought about Mike. Right there in that room. Thought about him swaying at the edge of every room I'd ever seen him in. The engine that wouldn't stop. I was already gone from the relationship by then. Or close. But that word — I carried it out of the session.

Electric orange thread through the chest cavity. Heart, lungs, brain. The same probe-and-iterate past the point of exhaustion. The same engine idling at full throttle when there's nothing left to burn. The same restless connection-making that doesn't quit even when the body wants to lie down.

I thought I was luckier than Mike. Better context, better timing, better packaging. A blog to put the intensity somewhere instead of swaying in a doorway. Maybe that's true.

Maybe luck is just the name we give the version of the same wiring that doesn't get called a problem.

---

I was forty when it finally landed.

An LLM on a screen. Something I said to it, something it reflected back. The recognition arrived the way recognition always does — not as logic, but as a punch that rearranges the architecture of a room you've been standing in your whole life.

*Like seeing myself in the mirror.*

  src="https://zackproser.b-cdn.net/images/llm-mirror.webp"

The same thing I'd run from in Mike. The same thing the ex named in that room. The same thing his parents saw before either of us had the language for it.

Three mirrors. The parents. The partner. The machine.

Each one showed me what I couldn't.
