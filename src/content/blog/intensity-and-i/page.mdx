I met him at Kenyon College — 1,500 kids total across four years, a tiny liberal arts campus in the middle of nowhere in Ohio, the kind of place where everyone knows your name by the second week whether you want them to or not.

His first name was Mike. But nobody called him that.

Someone started calling him "Intensity" early on, and it stuck the way things stick in small communities — not because it's kind, but because it's precise. He was large, tall, broad, with a physicality that felt like it took up more than his share of the room. He had energy that was impossible to ignore. And he desperately wanted to be included. To be part of the whole college experience, whatever that meant to the rest of us.

He was incredibly bright. Everyone said so, especially after he walked away — the qualifier that said everything. Bright but off. That was the phrase. You could see it in the way he'd try to join conversations, the way he'd show up to things and not quite land. In small groups he was a lot. People with the intensity moniker continued.

Over time he would start to come to the same parties that I went to. We were all getting completely wasted — that was the ambient state of early-2000s college social life, at least in my corner of it — and even when he wasn't at a party he would be standing there, that full massive frame jerking around and swaying, his eyes as red as cherry pie, sometimes chain-smoking too, drinking on top of it.

One of the strangest things was the manner in which it didn't seem like the immense amounts of marijuana and alcohol even affected his ability to reason or to focus or to make strong connections between concepts. He could hold a conversation that was genuinely interesting even while visibly wrecked. That was the part nobody knew what to do with. The cognitive machinery kept functioning underneath all of it. People with the intensity moniker continued.

I had a fledgling kind of friendship or social connection with him. I also had the whole blog post story — the one where you realize at 40 that you were exactly like him, but initially more socially graceful, better liked, while still carrying a kind of hostile dark brooding writer personal brand. We were all doing that at the time, trying on different hats and personas, building our identities out of curated rebellion and deliberate mystique. I had the social graces he lacked. But the underlying thing was the same.

Flash forward to a time in couples therapy. The therapist asked my ex-partner what her experience of me was, what she noticed, what came to mind. The first thing she said: I was very intense.

I remember that cutting and hurting. It landed like an accusation. An intensity marker I just kept returning to, because I thought of Intensity Mike. The whole thing collapsed into the same observation across two decades. Everyone had always said it about him, and now someone was saying it about me, and the recognition was immediate and total.

Later — in the same relationship — she said something else. I was completely baked out of my mind on an amount of high-grade marijuana that would have put other normal men my size on the hunt for EMTs because they would have thought they were dying, plus enough left over to stun a small mule. And she looked at me and said: "Wow, even when you're high as a kite, that mind just keeps on functioning properly, doesn't it?"

Two moments. The first one: the label. Intense. A verdict. The second one: the observation that the verdict was correct in a specific and measurable way. Even at the floor of consciousness, even with the throttle fully floored, the engine kept running.

I was diagnosed autistic at 36. Severe ADHD, also at 36. October 2025. The diagnosis explained things I'd spent my whole life not understanding — the way focus worked differently for me, the way intensity was not a choice but a structural feature. The interest-based nervous system. The paradoxical need for more stimulation to achieve calm. The background backpressure of a brain that never quite goes quiet.

Looking at Intensity Mike now, I see someone who had the same machinery I have, but without the social camouflage I was born with. I had better suppression capabilities and was more guarded socially. That was the only difference. He was loud and I was quiet. He was direct and I was calculating. The underlying architecture was identical.

This is a piece about coming to see the other as yourself. About recognizing the shape of your own experience in someone else's and understanding that the difference between them and you was never the machinery — it was only ever the packaging.
