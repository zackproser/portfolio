I was 19. College.

Mike stood outside every group, trying to get in, swaying.

Weed, beer, Marlboros. Eyes red as brake lights, locked on the people who weren&rsquo;t letting him in.

&ldquo;That guy&rsquo;s intense.&rdquo; &ldquo;Intense Mike.&rdquo;

The sports guys made it a nickname &mdash; &ldquo;Intensity&rdquo; as he walked up, &ldquo;Intensity&rdquo; as he walked away. Open secret he was awkward and bizarre. Painful for everyone.

We were all shitty to him. That&rsquo;s the story.

His parents visited freshman year. Both psychologists. They&rsquo;d known what their kid was for a long time.

His mom found me in the hallway. &ldquo;It looks like you and Mike are becoming friends.&rdquo; She asked for my phone number. It felt weird at the time. I gave it to her anyway, because I didn&rsquo;t have the language to say no.

Months later, after I&rsquo;d cooled on Mike, she called. &ldquo;Are you and Mike having a falling out?&rdquo;

I lied. Said no. The call itself was intense &mdash; like a clinician running an assessment on the campus friend. I didn&rsquo;t have the word yet.

&ldquo;He&rsquo;s very intense.&rdquo;

First word. Only one I remember. Said like a diagnosis. Maybe it was.

In that room I saw Mike at the edge of every party &mdash; the engine that wouldn&rsquo;t stop, the body that couldn&rsquo;t hide it. The same word my partner was using on me. The same word the sports guys had used on him.

Months later. Kids in bed. I was doing something for the family, stoned out of my mind, eyes red as brake lights.

She said, almost in passing: &ldquo;Even when you&rsquo;re high as a kite, that mind just keeps on functioning perfectly, doesn&rsquo;t it?&rdquo;

Yeah. Three hours folding into five, into twelve. The body goes quiet &mdash; hands, chair, the day. The bathroom said something at hour seven and was overruled. I&rsquo;m not really here.

That&rsquo;s what Mike did, too. Weed, beer, Marlboros stacked on top of each other, and his mind kept cataloguing, connecting, reciting whole novels he&rsquo;d read years before. The substances bounced off the wiring. Just like mine did.

Same engine. Different paint.

Not a wire. &ldquo;Wire&rdquo; was the polite name.

Gasoline and electricity, an orange line threaded through heart and lungs and brain. The endless desire to probe and iterate, past my own capacity to feel exhausted, past any ability to downshift. Exhausting and constant. The way I am.

Same thing ran through him. I just couldn&rsquo;t look.
