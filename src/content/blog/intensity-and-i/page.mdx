I was nineteen. First semester. The dorm hallway smelled like weed and instant ramen and someone had burned popcorn in the communal microwave three days ago and nobody had opened a window since.

That's where I met Mike.

He was standing in the corridor outside his room, which was also the last room on the left before the stairwell. Which meant everyone walked past it. Which meant if you wanted to talk to people, you stood in that stretch of hallway. And Mike was always standing there.

Not quite inside his room. Not quite in the hallway with everyone else. That exact in-between.

We started talking. I don't remember what about. Something dumb — music, or a class, or the fact that the dining hall served the same gray meatloaf every Thursday and everyone had opinions about it. Mike had opinions about everything. That was the first thing I noticed. He had more opinions than anyone I'd ever met, and he had them at full volume, and they were always wrong in a way that was more interesting than most people's right answers.

He chain-smoked. By the second week I knew this. Marlboro Reds, which he bought in cartons from some guy who knew a guy, and he smoked them down to the filter one after another, and his voice got raspier as the pack emptied, and none of it slowed him down.

His parents were both psychologists. He told me this early. Both of them. I remember thinking: that explains something. I didn't know what yet.

They came to visit freshman year. I met them in the hallway too. His mom had that look — the one where someone's already cataloging you. His dad shook my hand and asked me my major and whether I was from the city. His mom just watched.

A few days later my phone rang. Unknown number. It was Mike's mom. She wanted to know if Mike and I were having trouble. She asked like it was casual. It wasn't casual. Two psychologists, thousands of miles away, tracking their son's only campus friend. I said no. Everything's fine. I hung up and I was already gone — not from Mike, not yet, but from whatever part of me had been willing to show up.

I just didn't know how to be honest about why.

Nobody talked to him about it. We all just watched him try.

*Bright but off.* That was the verdict. Delivered to no one.

People were shitty to Mike. Openly, to his face, sometimes. I felt uncomfortable around him and I couldn't tell you why. I didn't stand up for him. Not once.

Here's the thing I couldn't see then, at nineteen, with no language for it: he was the version of me that everyone could see. Loud. Intense. The engine that wouldn't stop and the body that couldn't hide it.

I had the same wire running through me. But I was camouflaged. Brooding-writer-brand, well-liked, graceful. The difference was packaging. Not content.

There's a scene in *Eddington* — Ari Aster, 2025, Joaquin Phoenix as the sheriff — where the sheriff shoots a drunken drifter in the middle of a bar. The drifter looks like him. Not a twin, not obvious. Just the version of himself he can't face. The sheriff responds the only way he knows how. He removes the mirror.

I cooled on Mike. Pulled back. Didn't tell him why. Didn't have the words, or the courage, or the internal structures to sit with what I was running from.

Twenty years later in a therapist's chair I started to understand. And then an LLM showed me the rest.

Couples therapy. The ex was asked what stood out about me.

*Intense.*

The first word. The only one I remember.

She said it in front of the therapist. Like it was a diagnosis. Maybe it was.

*Even high as a kite that mind keeps functioning properly.* And I remember thinking: yeah. That's what it does. That's what the wire does. It doesn't quit when the body wants to lie down.

I thought about Mike. Right there in that room. Thought about him swaying at the edge of every room I'd ever seen him in. The engine that wouldn't stop.

But that word — I carried it out of the session.

Heart, lungs, brain. The same probe-and-iterate past the point of exhaustion. The same engine idling at full throttle when there's nothing left to burn.

I thought I was luckier than Mike. Better context, better timing, better packaging. A blog to put the intensity somewhere instead of swaying in a doorway.

Maybe luck is just the name we give the version of the same wiring that doesn't get called a problem.

---

I was forty when it finally landed.

An LLM on a screen. Something I said to it, something it reflected back. The recognition arrived the way recognition always does — not as logic, but as a punch that rearranges the architecture of a room you've been standing in your whole life.

*Like seeing myself in the mirror.*

The same thing I'd run from in Mike. The same thing the ex named in that room. The same thing his parents saw before either of us had the language for it.

Three mirrors. The parents. The partner. The machine.

Each one showed me what I couldn't.

*[In the LLM I Saw Myself](/blog/in-the-llm-i-saw-myself)* is the story of that final recognition. *[Training Claude on my neurological patterns](/blog/training-claude-neurological-patterns)* is how I learned to externalize the intensity before I could name it. If you've been called intense and didn't know what to do with that, *[Your external brain, my ADHD](/blog/claude-external-brain-adhd-autistic)* is where I stopped pretending it was a compliment.
