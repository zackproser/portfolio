There was a Mike at Kenyon.

Not a metaphor, not a composite — a real guy who showed up to the same parties I did, stood at the same edges, and who picked up the kind of nickname you don't shake. "Intensity." Sometimes just "Intense Mike," which was apparently how it landed in conversation most often. Both showed up, both got used, and he answered to both without flinching.

He was tall, broad-shouldered, white, with short brown hair and this gaze that cut across a room — the kind you noticed when it landed on you even if you weren't trying to. He wore a black leather jacket a lot, over jeans, with a button-down shirt or a t-shirt underneath depending on the night. The jacket was sort of his signature. When he walked into a room you knew it before you turned around.

I want to be precise about what I mean when I say he was large, tall, and broad, with intense physicality and energy — because the voice-dump I gave myself in the original telling of this was too abstract. The point isn't that he was big. The point is that he was *present* in a way that made smaller rooms feel smaller. His shoulders took up visual space. His eyes took up social space. And none of it was on purpose — that's the part people missed, or chose not to see.

The ridicule came later. At first it was just the nickname forming — the kind of thing that spreads through a friend group because it's useful shorthand, and nobody thinks very hard about what the shorthand costs the person it's applied to. But it didn't stay neutral. Eventually people said it to his face. Not the casual "oh, there's Intensity" but the pointed kind, the kind that's a joke at someone else's expense. He'd been labeled, and the label had weight, and the weight was structural.

He was bright but off. That's what people said when he walked away. Not to him — after. The way you'd describe an athlete who'd blown a play, except the play was existing in a room and the coaches were all of us.

At parties, he'd be standing there — this full massive frame just swaying, not quite moving with the rhythm of the room, not quite disconnected from it either. Eyes red as cherry pie. Chain-smoking sometimes, drinking on top of it. The whole time this energy rolling off him like heat, like he was running a low-grade engine at full throttle even when he was just standing around. And here was the thing nobody talked about but everyone noticed: all of it — the weed, the alcohol, the late nights, the constant presence — didn't seem to degrade his ability to reason. He could still make sharp connections, still follow a thread, still land an observation that made the room recalibrate. He was just *a lot* in small groups. Present in a way that didn't let you forget he was there. So people with the intensity moniker continued.

I had a loose social connection to him — not friends exactly, but the kind of thing where you nod when you see each other and there's an unspoken acknowledgment that you're both at the same party for the same reason. He wanted in. You could see it. He wasn't trying to be cool and he wasn't trying to be interesting. He just wanted to be part of whatever was happening in the room, and his presence kept making it awkward for everyone else. The thing about wanting something badly enough is that the wanting itself becomes visible, and visibility is the enemy of the casual ease that social acceptance requires.

---

I want to be clear about what I recognized later and when. At 40, I finally saw it.

I was exactly like him.

I want to say it differently so you hear it fresh: the *same*. Not similar. Not in the same ballpark. The exact same wiring running under different social packaging. I had the intensity — that low-grade engine at full throttle, that hyperfocus that doesn't shut off, that capacity to make connections and follow threads even when I'm also drinking or smoking or tired or whatever else. The same restless energy in a different costume.

I was just more socially graceful about it.

That sounds like I'm saying I was better than him, and I'm not. I'm saying I was luckier. I had better timing, better context, better support, and probably some genetic roll of the dice that made the same trait read as "moody and interesting" in me instead of "too much." I had a blog, and a personal brand — hostile, dark, brooding writer, which was very much a thing you could be at 22 without it being a cry for help — and the brand gave me somewhere to put the intensity. It had somewhere to *go* instead of just standing there swaying in a doorway. The energy got channeled into content instead of into social flailing.

Mike didn't have that. Mike just had the energy and no good way to deploy it in a room full of people who were already looking for reasons to call him Intensity to his face.

---

Couples therapy. My ex was asked what stood out about me, what she'd noticed in the relationship, and the first word out of her mouth was: *intense.*

I remember the cut of it. Not because I disagreed — I've always known I'm intense, that's not news — but because of where my mind went immediately. Right to Mike. Right to the moniker. Right to the image of him swaying at the edge of a party, eyes red, looking like he was running a fever that nobody else could see. I thought about it for a long time after that session.

Later in the same relationship, on a night when I'd smoked an amount of high-grade marijuana that would have put most people on the floor — the kind of amount where normal men my size would be calling EMTs, plus enough left over to stun a small mule — my ex looked at me and said, with what I can only describe as genuine bewilderment: "Wow. Even when you're high as a kite, that mind just keeps on functioning properly, doesn't it?"

The same thing. The exact same observation people used to make about Mike when he walked away. *Bright but off.* Or in this case: *off the charts high, still fully operational.* The engine doesn't stop. It never stops.

---

I know what I am now. Diagnosed autism and ADHD at 36 — October 2025. Interest-based nervous system, priority blindness, the brain radio that won't tune to anything except what's currently on fire. None of this is news anymore, it's just information I've been given official clearance to act on.

What I didn't have until I was 40 was the recognition. Not just "I'm intense" — I've always known that — but: *I am the same person Mike was.* Same wiring. Same engine. Same way of being in a room that doesn't quite fit the shape the room wants you to be. The difference was context and packaging and luck. The difference was that my intensity had a brand and Mike's didn't. The difference was that I learned to put it somewhere productive and Mike was still standing at the edge of the party when I thought about him.

Coming to see the other as myself — that's what this piece has always been about. Not "intensity is bad" or "intensity is good." Just: there was one of us in the room, and there was me, and we were the same. The "Intensity" moniker was never about Mike being too much. It was about a room not knowing what to do with someone who was running the same software they were, just louder.

I know what I am now. I knew it then, too, I just couldn't see it yet.

*"Intensity" and I.* The title was always about the reunion.
