### What to Dictate

**Episode description (2-3 paragraphs):** Talk through what you covered, why it matters, and who should listen. This takes about 2 minutes of dictation and produces better copy than 20 minutes of typing, because you're speaking with the same energy you just had in the episode.

**Timestamps and highlights:** "At 12 minutes we talked about X, at 25 minutes the guest shared their framework for Y, at 40 minutes we got into the controversial take on Z." Dictate these while scanning your recording timeline.

**Social media posts:** Dictate 3-4 social posts highlighting different moments from the episode. Speaking produces more natural social copy than typing — it sounds like something a human would actually say.

**Guest bio and links:** If your guest mentioned resources, tools, or their own projects during the episode, dictate them while you still remember. "Sarah mentioned her new book on distributed systems, it's on O'Reilly, and she also recommended the Jepsen blog for testing databases."

## Why Not Just Transcribe the Episode?

Transcription gives you everything that was said. Show notes require editorial judgment about what matters, what order to present it in, and how to frame it for someone who hasn't listened yet. That's a writing task, not a transcription task.

Your voice dictation of the show notes includes the editorial layer: you're naturally selecting highlights, adding context, and framing the conversation — because that's what humans do when they tell someone about a conversation they just had.

## The Numbers

- Average show notes: 400-800 words
- Typing at 50 WPM: 8-16 minutes of pure typing, plus thinking time → usually 30-45 minutes total
- Dictating at 150 WPM: 3-5 minutes of speaking, plus 5-10 minutes of cleanup → 8-15 minutes total

For weekly podcasters, that's 2+ hours saved per month on show notes alone. Add social posts and newsletter blurbs, and you're saving a full working day per month.

## Batch Production Days

If you record multiple episodes in a batch (many podcasters record 2-4 per session), dictating show notes between episodes keeps your recall fresh and prevents the "I need to write notes for 4 episodes" backlog that kills podcasts.

Record episode 1 → dictate notes → 5 minute break → record episode 2 → dictate notes. Each set of notes takes 5-10 minutes while everything is fresh, versus a painful multi-hour writing session days later.
