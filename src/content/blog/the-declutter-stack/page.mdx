The problem with the standard advice to "just clean more" is that it assumes the person has the executive function to initiate cleaning in the first place. They don't. Neither do you.

This is not a post about aesthetics. This is not a post about minimalism. The goal is not less stuff — it is fewer decisions about stuff.

The goal is to build systems where the behavior you want requires near-zero initiation energy. The environment does the work for you. Every object has a designated home. Every recurring task has a pre-built path. You stop managing low-level decisions so your brain can handle the ones that actually matter.

For ADHD brains, this is not a preference. It is infrastructure.

## The Cortisol-Clutter Loop

A [Psychology Today article](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/basis/cortisol-clutter-connection) from 2024 describes a mechanism discovered in a 2010 study of dual-income couples: wives in cluttered homes showed *rising* cortisol throughout the day. In organized homes, cortisol dropped naturally without intervention.

The mechanism is straightforward. Constant low-level noticing of what needs to be done → cognitive load → suppressed motivation → more clutter → higher cortisol. The cluttered environment is not the cause of the stress. The cluttered environment is the thing that *sustains* the stress, all day, without you doing anything about it.

For ADHD brains already running on low baseline dopamine, this loop is especially expensive. There is less executive reserve to fight it. The clutter does not just sit there — it actively drains you.

## Monotropism and the Attention Tunnel

While you are reading this, your attention is a narrow beam. This is not a metaphor. Research into monotropism — the theory developed by Sarah Murray, Erica Lawson, and subsequently expanded by Philo Dwyer, Ruth Williams, and Dinah Rivera — describes neurodivergent minds as having a narrower, deeper "attention tunnel." When multiple stimuli compete for attention, the tunnel does not just narrow. It ruptures.

A cluttered visual field is not neutral noise. It actively prevents the depth of focus ADHD and autistic brains are built for. You are not being distracted by the clutter — the clutter is preventing you from doing the thing you sat down to do.

An organized, minimal environment is not a preference. For many neurodivergent brains, it is a prerequisite for the kind of thinking required to get anything done.

## Point of Performance

Russell Barkley's foundational ADHD model identifies a concept called **point of performance**: the environment at the moment of intended action is where behavior change actually happens.

"Clean more" is a statement made at the wrong point in time. It assumes that when the moment to clean arrives, you will have the executive function to initiate the action. You will not. The instruction ignored the executive function required to *start* cleaning in the first place.

The solution is not to try harder. The solution is to design the moment of performance so that the required action is already set up.

Labeled drawers. A dedicated hook for keys. A home for everything. When putting something away requires near-zero initiation energy, the behavior changes not because you willed it, but because the environment made it automatic. You did not decide to set up the moka pot — it is already on the counter, ready to go. You did not decide to fill the thermos — it was filled that morning as part of a batch system.

The moka pot example is infrastructure. Not indulgence.

## User Agency Reduces Cognitive Load

A 2025 workspace study by Corgan, conducted in partnership with the University of Arkansas using mobile EEG and wearable sensors, found something worth sitting with: user control over immediate environment — light height, desk position, acoustic control — measurably reduced cognitive load and stress.

Not just for ADHD brains. The effect generalized across all participants.

Every system you build that gives you *control* or *predictability* is a direct load offloader. The adjustable bed that finally works reliably — that is agency. The thermoses filled in the morning — that is a system that removes the per-cup decision, every cup, forever. The Bluetooth mouse and keyboard for the gaming rig — that eliminates the cable-management decision permanently, from both the setup and the cleanup.

The office disorganization is probably the most expensive clutter in your life. It is where the cognitive work happens. It is where the decisions get made. It is the place where your thinking is supposed to happen — and it is full of competing stimuli that are silently rupturing your attention tunnel.

## What a System Actually Looks Like

When I opened the boxes from the recent decluttering session, the act of finding a home for each thing was not aesthetic work. It was systems work. Every item that gets put away permanently is a decision you will never have to make again. The act of *finding a home for things* is the system. The home is the output.

The multiple thermoses and the one large one: batch-making tea in the morning, distributing into thermoses, done for the day. Not a charming domestic habit — a system that eliminates the per-cup cognitive transaction every single time you want a drink. The decision to have tea has been separated from the decision to make tea. The moka pot on the counter: the same principle. The thing you want is already set up. You do not have to initiate the setup.

This is the pattern: separate the decision from the action. Remove the per-occurrence choice. Build the behavior into the environment.

## The Frame That Matters

You are not a minimalist. You are a systems thinker.

The goal is not less stuff. It is fewer decisions about stuff. Every decision you eliminate from your day is executive function that stays available for the things that actually require it.

The external environment, designed correctly, becomes an extension of working memory. You are not remembering where things are. You are trusting the system. And the system does not forget.

That is the declutter stack. Not a clean room. An operating environment.
