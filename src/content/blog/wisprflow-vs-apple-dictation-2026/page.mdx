For two years, I used Apple Dictation as my primary voice input method. It was free, it was built into macOS, and I didn't have to think about it. My workflow was simple: hit the dictation shortcut, speak, watch the text appear.

Then I spent a week with WisprFlow and deleted the Apple Dictation shortcut from my menu bar. Here's what changed.

## What Apple Dictation Gets Right

Apple Dictation has improved significantly since the early days. On macOS Ventura and later, Enhanced Dictation gives you offline transcription — no internet required, and no audio going to Apple's servers. The accuracy for everyday English is solid.

It's always there. Menu bar shortcut, keyboard shortcut, global availability across every application. Zero setup, zero cost.

For basic voice-to-text — jotting down a quick note, adding a comment, transcribing a short passage — Apple Dictation is competent.

## Where Apple Dictation Falls Short

Apple Dictation is fundamentally a text-input tool. It takes what you say and converts it to text. That's it. There's no smart punctuation, no automatic formatting, no context awareness.

Say "dear comma john colon new line" and you get exactly that — the words "dear comma john colon new line" — because Apple Dictation transcribes literally. Dragon NaturallySpeaking and WisprFlow both handle punctuation intelligently. You say "Dear John," and you get "Dear John," with the comma and the salutation capitalized properly.
