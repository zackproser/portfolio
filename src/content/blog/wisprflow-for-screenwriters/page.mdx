Screenwriting is one of the few professions where the gap between thinking and typing actively kills the work. You hear the dialogue in your head — the rhythm, the pacing, the way a character drops a line — and by the time you've typed it out, the voice is gone. You're editing syntax instead of channeling character.

Voice dictation fixes this. Speak the dialogue as you hear it. The words land on the page with the cadence you intended, not the cadence your typing speed allowed.

### Dialogue Sounds Better Spoken

This is the obvious one, but it matters more than people think. When you type dialogue, you unconsciously optimize for what looks right on screen. When you speak it, you optimize for what sounds right out loud — which is what dialogue actually needs to do.

### Coverage and Script Notes

If you work in development — reading scripts and writing coverage — voice dictation is a game-changer. Read the script, then dictate your coverage notes while your reactions are fresh. Synopsis, comments on structure, character notes, and your recommend/consider/pass verdict, all captured at conversation speed instead of hunt-and-peck.

## The Speed Difference

Typing speed for most writers: 40-60 WPM. WisprFlow dictation: 150-180 WPM. For a 120-page screenplay at roughly 250 words per page, that's 30,000 words. At 50 WPM typing, that's 10 hours of pure keystroke time. At 150 WPM dictation, it's about 3.3 hours.

You're not going to dictate an entire screenplay in one pass — you'll still revise, restructure, and rewrite. But the first draft velocity matters enormously for maintaining creative momentum.

### How It Works in Practice

1. Open Final Draft (or whatever you use)
2. Hit WisprFlow's hotkey to start dictating
3. Speak your scene — dialogue, action lines, parentheticals
4. WisprFlow transcribes in real time with punctuation and formatting
5. Review, polish, move to the next scene

The key insight: you're not dictating a perfect draft. You're capturing the raw creative output while it's alive, then sculpting it afterward. Separation of generation and editing is the oldest writing advice there is — voice dictation just makes it practical.
