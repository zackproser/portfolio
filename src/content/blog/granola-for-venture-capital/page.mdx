# Granola for Venture Capital: Due Diligence Meetings

Venture capital runs on information. The partner who remembers exactly what a founder said about their churn rate six months ago—in a 30-minute pitch on a Tuesday in a week with 40 other meetings—has a fundamental advantage over the one reconstructing from vague notes and memory.

That's the promise of Granola for VC: not just meeting notes, but a searchable, intelligent layer of memory across every founder conversation, due diligence call, LP update, and board meeting. Here's how top VC workflows are changing now that AI-powered meeting documentation is actually good.

## The VC Meeting Volume Problem

Let's start with the scale of the challenge. A general partner at an active early-stage fund might sit in:

- 5-10 founder pitch meetings per week
- 2-4 portfolio company syncs
- 1-2 LP calls or updates
- Weekly partner/IC meetings
- Multiple due diligence calls per active deal
- Board meetings for portfolio companies (2-4 per board seat per quarter)
- LP AGMs, conferences, scout calls, reference checks

That's 50-80+ meetings per month, each generating unique information that should inform investment decisions, portfolio support, and LP relationships. The cognitive load is staggering. No one's memory is good enough to hold all of it without infrastructure.

The traditional solution is a notes system of some kind—a Google Doc, a Notion database, a Salesforce-connected ATS. The problem is that notes are only as good as the person taking them. When you're in the middle of a founder pitch, trying to simultaneously evaluate the person, assess the market, track the numbers, and formulate probing questions, there's not much bandwidth left for documentation.

Granola solves this by handling the documentation layer automatically, so partners can be fully present in the meeting while still walking away with complete, structured notes.

## Capturing Founder Pitch Details That Actually Matter

Founder pitches are information-dense and emotionally charged. A good pitch covers market opportunity, product differentiation, traction metrics, business model, team background, competitive landscape, and funding ask—all in 30-45 minutes. The best founders also reveal things between the lines: how they handle hard questions, what they're worried about, where they're still figuring things out.

Granola captures all of it. The structured notes from a founder pitch include:

**Market and problem statement**: What market is the founder addressing, what's the size claim, what problem are they solving, and how do they frame the urgency?

**Product and differentiation**: What specifically does the product do, what's the core technical or product insight, what makes this defensible?

**Traction data**: ARR, MRR, growth rate, cohort retention, NPS, logo count—whatever metrics the founder shared, captured accurately with the context of how they were presented.

**Team composition**: Founder backgrounds, how the team met, key hires made, roles still open. The "why this team" narrative.

**Business model and unit economics**: Revenue model, CAC, LTV, payback period, burn rate, runway.

**Funding history and ask**: What's been raised, from whom, at what valuation, and what's the current ask targeting what milestone.

**Red flags and open questions**: The things that didn't quite add up, the questions you asked and the answers you got (or didn't get), the areas where you want to do more digging.

This level of detail in structured notes transforms the follow-up process. Instead of scheduling a second call to re-cover ground from the first meeting, you can advance the conversation based on what you already know—which signals to founders that you've done your homework and take their time seriously.

## Due Diligence Documentation Across Multiple Stakeholders

Due diligence on a serious deal involves a lot of conversations: customer reference calls, technical deep dives with the engineering team, market expert calls, legal review, financial model review, competitor analysis calls. A typical Series A diligence process might involve 15-25 discrete calls over 4-6 weeks.

Each call adds a piece to the picture. The challenge is synthesizing those pieces into a coherent investment thesis when the calls happened over weeks, involved different partners and associates, and touched different dimensions of the business.

Granola's meeting notes become due diligence artifacts. Every reference call is documented with the customer's exact language about what they love, what they wish was different, and whether they'd expand their contract. Every technical call captures the engineering team's architecture decisions and technical debt profile. Every expert call captures the market dynamics from someone who's lived in the space.

When it's time to write the IC memo, the notes are searchable and comprehensive. You're not synthesizing from memory or fragmentary typed notes—you're working from complete documentation of everything you learned across every call.

**Reference call documentation workflow with Granola:**
1. Reference call runs in Google Meet or Zoom
2. Granola captures the full transcript and generates structured notes
3. Notes include: relationship to company, tenure as customer, use case, satisfaction indicators, expansion intent, any concerns raised
4. Associate tags notes to the deal in your deal tracking system
5. Pattern matching across multiple references reveals consistent themes

When three out of four references mention the same concern about implementation complexity, that's a data point that shapes how you think about the deal. Granola makes those patterns visible across your full due diligence call set.

## Portfolio Company Review Meeting Notes

Board membership means being a useful strategic partner to your portfolio companies, not just a vote on major decisions. The partners who provide the most value to founders are the ones who track company progress closely enough to ask sharp questions, surface relevant pattern matches from other portfolio companies, and remember what was discussed in the last meeting.

Granola applied to portfolio company reviews creates a longitudinal record of company progress. Over 12-18 months of quarterly board meetings, you build a searchable history of:

- Metrics at each milestone (revenue, headcount, burn rate, key KPIs)
- Strategic discussions and decisions made
- Challenges surfaced and how they were addressed
- Commitments made by management and whether they were met
- Patterns in how the team operates under pressure

This longitudinal view is invaluable when a portfolio company hits a rough patch. Instead of trying to reconstruct from memory whether the current team execution problem is new or has been a pattern, you can review the meeting notes and answer the question factually. That's better for the company and better for your decision-making about where to deploy additional support or have harder conversations.

The notes also make you a better board member in real time. When you can quickly review what was discussed last quarter before a board meeting, you walk in with continuity and context. Founders notice when their board members remember their business.

## LP Communication and Quarterly Update Documentation

LP relationships are long-term partnerships that depend on communication quality and consistency. Quarterly updates, annual meetings, fund close calls, and special communications all require preparation and follow-up documentation.

Granola for LP communications creates records of:

**Quarterly update calls**: What metrics were presented, how LPs responded, questions raised, commitments made for follow-up. If an LP asked specifically about the performance of portfolio company X, that's documented—so you can proactively update them when something changes with X.

**Annual meeting notes**: The full conversation from your AGM, including LP concerns raised, portfolio company highlights discussed, and fund strategy questions answered.

**Fundraising call notes**: During a fund raise, you're having dozens of conversations with potential LPs. Each one has specific interests, concerns, and relationship context. Granola notes from each conversation make it possible to personalize follow-up communications meaningfully rather than sending generic materials.

**One-on-one LP conversations**: The informal check-ins and relationship calls that inform how LPs are thinking about their portfolio and whether they'll re-up in your next fund.

The LPs who feel most engaged with a fund are the ones who feel heard and remembered. Detailed notes make that possible at scale—you can have 50 LP relationships and still remember that LP X cares especially about climate tech and LP Y wants updates on the SaaS metrics in your portfolio.

## IC Meeting Notes and Decision Tracking

Investment committee meetings are where deals live or die. The full partner discussion of a deal—the bears and bulls cases, the concerns raised and how they were addressed, the final vote and the reasoning—should be documented precisely because these decisions have real consequences and need to be understood in retrospect.

Granola in IC meetings creates a decision record. If you made an investment and it doesn't go as planned, the IC notes from the original decision tell you what you knew at the time, what concerns were raised, and what logic drove the final decision. That's valuable for post-mortems and improving your decision-making framework.

It's also valuable for deals that come back around. A founder you passed on two years ago may be back for their Series B at a company that's grown substantially. The IC notes from when you saw them before tell you exactly what your concerns were and whether they've been addressed.

**What to capture in IC meeting notes:**
- Deal overview recap (brief)
- Bull case presented (usually by the deal lead)
- Bear case and specific concerns raised by other partners
- Key due diligence findings that address or validate concerns
- Financial terms and comparables discussed
- Final decision and voting record
- Post-decision action items (additional diligence, term sheet preparation, pass letter)

The discipline of documenting IC decisions also makes you more rigorous in the process itself. When you know the conversation is being captured and you'll be able to review it later, the quality of the analysis tends to improve.

## Competitive Landscape Research Call Notes

Market understanding is a competitive advantage in VC. Partners who have deep, current knowledge of how different sectors are evolving make better investment decisions and provide better portfolio support. That knowledge often comes from conversations—with domain experts, former operators, research analysts, founders in adjacent spaces.

These calls are high-signal and difficult to document in real time. You're asking probing questions, listening carefully, and trying to synthesize implications—not taking notes.

Granola captures these conversations so the knowledge isn't lost. Expert call notes from a series of conversations about, say, the enterprise AI infrastructure market become a body of intelligence you can search and reference when evaluating deals in that space.

The notes also become useful context for portfolio companies. When a portfolio company asks you about the competitive dynamics in their market, you can search your Granola notes from relevant expert calls and provide specific, sourced perspectives rather than general impressions.

## Deal Flow Pipeline Documentation: Meeting to Term Sheet

The deal flow pipeline in VC involves multiple touchpoints over weeks or months before a deal reaches IC. Granola creates a continuous documentation thread through the entire process:

**Initial outreach/intro meeting**: First impressions, preliminary assessment, specific things to explore further.

**Follow-up deep dive**: Addressed concerns from first meeting, new information surfaced, updated view.

**Due diligence call series**: Each call documented and tagged to the deal.

**Reference calls**: Customer, employee, and expert perspectives.

**Management presentation/site visit**: Full documentation of formal presentation.

**IC preparation**: Partner pre-discussion notes, concerns to address, questions outstanding.

**IC meeting**: Decision record.

**Term sheet negotiation**: Key terms discussed, negotiation history.

This complete documentation thread makes the whole deal process reviewable. For deals that close, it becomes part of the investment file. For deals that don't, it's a record of what you learned that might be relevant to the next deal in the space.
