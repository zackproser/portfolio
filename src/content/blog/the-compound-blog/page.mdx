## The math: linear vs. compounding

As a consultant, I earn $X per hour. If I stop working, I stop earning. The relationship between effort and income is perfectly linear — one hour of work produces one hour of pay, forever.

A blog post works differently. I invest time once — say, 30 minutes to an hour — and that post generates traffic, affiliate clicks, and audience growth for months or years afterward. The marginal cost of serving that post to one more reader is effectively zero. There's no additional work required. The post just sits there, doing its job.

When you have 500+ posts doing this simultaneously, the effect compounds. Each new post adds to the total surface area of the site. It creates new entry points from search engines. It creates new internal links that strengthen other posts. It gives AI chatbots more content to cite. The whole catalog works together.

My Pinecone vs. Weaviate comparison has generated affiliate revenue every single month since I published it. My AI code editor roundup drives traffic from dozens of different search queries. My infrastructure posts attract readers who then discover my tool comparisons. Everything feeds everything else.

## How AI assistants changed the economics

The economics of blogging shifted dramatically when I started using AI assistants to help with content production. Here's what my current workflow looks like:

I message Hermes — my AI assistant running on an EC2 instance — through Discord. I describe the post I want: the topic, the angle, key points to cover. Hermes writes the MDX, generates a hero image, uploads it to Bunny CDN, commits everything to git, and opens a pull request. I review the draft, make edits if needed, and merge. The whole process takes me 15-20 minutes of active effort per post.

I wrote about this workflow in detail in [How My AI Assistant Ships Blog Posts](/blog/how-my-ai-assistant-ships-blog-posts).

Before Hermes, a blog post took me 2-4 hours of focused writing time. I could publish maybe two posts per week at that pace, and it required dedicated blocks of time at my desk. Now I can direct multiple posts per day from my phone while doing other things.

The marginal cost per post has dropped to near zero. My time investment is 15-20 minutes of review and direction. The infrastructure cost — EC2, CDN, API calls — is negligible compared to the revenue each post generates. I'm producing more content with less effort, and each piece of content compounds on top of everything else.

## The flywheel

Here's how the compounding works in practice:

**Search traffic grows with volume.** More posts mean more indexed pages, more keywords covered, more entry points from Google. My site ranks for thousands of long-tail queries I never explicitly targeted. Each new post has a chance of ranking for queries I didn't anticipate.

**Internal linking compounds authority.** When I publish a new comparison post, I link it to related posts that already exist. Those links strengthen both pages. A reader who arrives on one post discovers three more through internal links. Average session depth increases, which signals quality to search engines, which improves rankings, which drives more traffic.

**AI citations multiply with catalog size.** AI chatbots cite my content more because I have more content to cite. When someone asks a chatbot about any developer tool I've covered, there's a good chance one of my posts is specific enough to be referenced. The larger the catalog, the wider the citation surface.

**Affiliate earnings scale with traffic.** More traffic means more clicks on comparison tables and tool recommendations. I don't do hard sells — I write honest comparisons and include affiliate links where relevant. The earnings are a natural byproduct of useful content at scale.

**Email subscribers grow the audience.** Each post is a chance to convert a reader into a subscriber. Subscribers see new posts, share them, and return to the site. That organic distribution amplifies every future post.

## What this looks like month over month

In January, I published 15 posts. In February, those 15 posts continued generating traffic alongside the 12 I published that month. By March, I had three months of compounding content working simultaneously, plus everything I'd written before that.

The revenue curve isn't a straight line. It bends upward because each month's content adds to the total. A post published in January might peak in traffic in February as it gets indexed and ranked, then settle into a steady baseline that continues for months. Multiply that by hundreds of posts and you get a revenue floor that rises over time even if I stop publishing tomorrow.

I won't stop publishing, though. The ROI on each new post is too good when the production cost is 15-20 minutes of review.

## Why most people don't do this

The barrier used to be time. Writing a good technical blog post took hours. Maintaining a publishing cadence of several posts per week was a full-time job on top of your actual full-time job. Most people burned out after a few months.

AI assistants removed that barrier. The effort per post dropped from hours to minutes. The quality stayed high because you're still the one with the expertise, opinions, and real-world experience — the AI handles the mechanical work of drafting, formatting, and asset management.

The remaining barrier is consistency. You have to actually publish, week after week, month after month. Not every post will be a hit. Some will get minimal traffic. But across hundreds of posts, the law of large numbers works in your favor. A handful of posts will drive disproportionate traffic and revenue, and you can't predict which ones in advance.

My approach: publish a lot, write about what I genuinely know and care about, let the catalog compound. The revenue engine I describe in [The Revenue Engine](/blog/the-revenue-engine) runs on this exact principle.
