## The Documentation Problem in Bankruptcy Practice

Bankruptcy cases generate a specific kind of documentation pressure. Unlike transactional work where the record lives in drafts and redlines, bankruptcy practice is meeting-heavy. The case develops through:

- **341 meetings**: Creditors question the debtor under oath, generating testimony that may matter throughout the case
- **Creditor committee meetings**: Complex multi-party discussions about case strategy, plan terms, asset valuations
- **Plan negotiation sessions**: Back-and-forth between debtor and creditor counsel where positions shift and commitments get made
- **Client consultations**: Distressed debtors processing complicated decisions about assets, claims, plan confirmation options
- **Expert witness prep**: Discussions with financial advisors, valuation experts, industry consultants
- **Internal strategy meetings**: Partner/associate case planning where the whole team needs to be aligned

The details from all of these conversations feed into filings, schedules, plan documents, and litigation strategy. Getting them wrong — or losing them — creates real problems.

The traditional solutions all have costs. Associate note-takers are expensive and add an intermediary. Recording everything creates privilege and admissibility issues. Taking your own notes means you're not fully present for the substance of the discussion. Writing from memory hours later means losing precision exactly when you need it.

### 341 Meeting Preparation and Debrief

341 meetings move fast. The trustee asks questions. Creditors appear with their own counsel. The debtor answers under oath. You're managing your client, tracking what's been said, watching for creditor counsel who might be building toward a nondischargeability complaint, and simultaneously trying to remember everything for your post-meeting debrief.

Granola captures your preparation sessions with the debtor — the answers you've rehearsed, the issues you've flagged, the documents you've organized. Then for the post-meeting debrief, you can walk through what happened verbally and have Granola structure that into case notes while it's still fresh.

"341 meeting complete. Trustee focused primarily on the real property transfers from 2022 — flagged both transactions for additional documentation. That's a complete post-341 case note in 45 seconds of verbal debrief.

### Creditor Negotiation Capture

Granola keeps that record. You capture negotiation calls with the consent of all parties (check your jurisdiction's rules), and you have an accurate contemporaneous record of what was said, by whom, and when. That record protects you when the other side's recollection conveniently shifts.

## Practical Considerations for Legal Use

Granola captures and processes meeting audio on-device and in the cloud. Most firms are developing policies on this — it's worth having the conversation.

A few practices that work well in legal contexts:

- Treat the AI notes as a first draft requiring your professional judgment, not a finished work product
