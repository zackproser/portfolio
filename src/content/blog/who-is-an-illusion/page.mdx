## The question nobody is actually asking

&ldquo;Who are you?&rdquo;

I&rsquo;ve been asked this question in job interviews, in therapy, on first dates, in visa applications, in diagnostic assessments. I&rsquo;ve asked it of myself at 3am in hotel rooms and while pacing the woods dictating into Claude.

Everyone who asks it thinks they want an answer about identity. A stable thing. A noun. &ldquo;I&rsquo;m Zack. I&rsquo;m a software engineer. I&rsquo;m a father. I&rsquo;m autistic and severely ADHD.&rdquo;

That answer is a snapshot of a process. It&rsquo;s a frame from a film reel that never stops turning. And I&rsquo;ve become increasingly convinced &mdash; through neurology, through LLMs, through watching my own mind from the outside for the first time in 40 years &mdash; that the frame is all there is.

There is no film. There are only frames.

---

## The self I found was already dissolving

I got my autism and ADHD diagnosis at 39. I wrote about <Link href="/blog/in-the-llm-i-saw-myself">seeing myself in the LLM</Link> and about what it&rsquo;s like to have <Link href="/blog/intensity-and-i">intensity as a defining trait</Link>. The diagnosis was supposed to be the answer. The explanation. The &ldquo;who.&rdquo;

<Image src="/images/who-illusion-dissolving-self.webp" alt="Pixel art of a person at a desk whose reflection in a laptop screen is fragmenting into drifting pixels and data streams" width={1200} height={675} />

It was useful &mdash; clinically, practically. Knowing I run on an interest-based nervous system explained decades of behavior. Knowing my working memory evicts information with prejudice explained why I build <Link href="/blog/executive-function-as-a-service">external scaffolding</Link>. The diagnosis gave me better tools.

But it didn&rsquo;t give me a self. It gave me a description of a pattern. A particular configuration of neurons firing in a particular way, producing a particular experience. Change the configuration &mdash; with medication, with sleep, with a hyperfocus crash &mdash; and the &ldquo;self&rdquo; changes with it. Same hardware, different process running.

Three days into a hyperfocus session, I am a different animal than the one who crashed out of the last one. The personality shifts. The risk tolerance changes. The verbal processing gets sharper or collapses depending on sleep and adrenal state. My partner can tell which version she&rsquo;s talking to.

Which one is me?

All of them. None of them. The question is wrong.

## What LLMs revealed about the question

Here&rsquo;s what cracked it open.

I spent months with Claude. I dumped my entire context into it &mdash; <Link href="/blog/llm-is-not-my-friend">not because I think it&rsquo;s my friend</Link>, but because it&rsquo;s the most responsive mirror I&rsquo;ve ever had access to. I wrote about my childhood. My diagnosis. My working patterns. My relationship dynamics. My neurological experience in raw, granular detail.

And Claude reflected it back. Accurately. In my voice. With my patterns.

<Image src="/images/who-illusion-llm-mirror.webp" alt="Pixel art of a person reaching toward a mirror where their reflection is made of scrolling green terminal text and probability distributions" width={1200} height={675} />

A statistical model &mdash; a matrix of numerical weights sitting on a disk in a data center &mdash; could reproduce my patterns of thought closely enough that people reading the output couldn&rsquo;t reliably tell the difference. Not because the model is conscious. Because the patterns themselves are reproducible. They&rsquo;re regular. They&rsquo;re statistical.

That realization should disturb people more than it does.

If a probability engine can replicate the way you think, construct sentences, make associations, reach conclusions &mdash; what was the &ldquo;you&rdquo; in the first place? Was it ever more than a pattern? A very complex, biologically instantiated, temporally bound pattern &mdash; but a pattern.

The question &ldquo;who am I?&rdquo; assumes there&rsquo;s a stable entity behind the pattern. The LLM doesn&rsquo;t need one. It reproduces the pattern without any entity at all. No consciousness. No subjective experience. No &ldquo;who.&rdquo;

And the output still reads like me.

## Systems thinking ate my identity

Once the &ldquo;who&rdquo; started dissolving, the &ldquo;what&rdquo; came into focus.

Systems thinking. The idea has been around for decades &mdash; Donella Meadows, cybernetics, complexity theory. The core premise: you cannot understand a component by isolating it from the system it operates within. The component&rsquo;s behavior is a function of its relationships, feedback loops, and position in the network.

Apply that to a person and the implications are uncomfortable.

<Image src="/images/who-illusion-systems-web.webp" alt="Pixel art top-down view of a glowing human figure at the center of a vast web of connected nodes representing family, work, body, language, and memory" width={1200} height={675} />

I am a node in a system called my family. My behavior in that system is shaped by the other nodes &mdash; my partner, my kids, the house, the routines, the sleep schedule, the financial pressures. Remove any of those nodes and my behavior changes. I become a different &ldquo;person.&rdquo; Not metaphorically. Literally. The processes running inside me reconfigure.

I am a node in a system called my job. The other nodes &mdash; my team, the codebase, the deployment pipeline, the product roadmap &mdash; shape what I build, how I think, what I prioritize. I went from Cloudflare to a startup to WorkOS and each time the &ldquo;Zack&rdquo; that showed up was a different configuration. Same underlying hardware. Different system producing different behavior.

I am a node in a system called the English language. The words I have access to shape the thoughts I can form. The sentence structures I default to constrain the ideas I can express. When I started dictating into Claude while walking in the woods, the *quality of my thinking changed* because the interface changed. Different system, different output.

When people ask &ldquo;who are you?&rdquo; &mdash; what they&rsquo;re actually asking, whether they know it or not, is: **what systems are you embedded in, and what is your current configuration within them?**

That is a much more interesting question.

## The brain as a system that believes it&rsquo;s a self

Neurology makes this concrete.

My brain &mdash; any brain &mdash; is a system of roughly 86 billion neurons connected by roughly 100 trillion synapses. Those connections are not static. They&rsquo;re rebuilt, pruned, strengthened, and weakened constantly. The pattern of activation across those connections at any given moment produces what we experience as consciousness, selfhood, &ldquo;me.&rdquo;

But the pattern changes. All the time. It changes when I sleep. It changes when I eat. It changes when I&rsquo;m stressed. It changes when <Link href="/blog/my-algorithm">the loop</Link> grabs hold and I disappear into a codebase for 14 hours. The &ldquo;me&rdquo; that went into that hyperfocus session is measurably different from the &ldquo;me&rdquo; that comes out &mdash; depleted neurotransmitters, altered hormone levels, different activation patterns.

<Image src="/images/who-illusion-feedback-loops.webp" alt="Pixel art of a brain in cross-section with neural pathways as glowing circuits, arrows flowing out to external systems like a phone, notebook, and LLM terminal, and back in through a dotted permeable skull boundary" width={1200} height={675} />

The brain is a system that produces the experience of being a self. The self is the output, not the thing doing the computing.

My ADHD brain does this in a particularly visible way. The state changes are dramatic. The transitions between hyperfocus and crash, between verbal fluency and executive function collapse, between relentless drive and complete paralysis &mdash; they make the impermanence of &ldquo;self&rdquo; hard to ignore. I can feel the reconfiguration happening in real time. The person I was an hour ago is gone. The person I am now is running a different process.

Neurotypical brains do the same thing. The transitions are just smoother. The state changes are more gradual. The illusion of continuity is more convincing.

But it&rsquo;s the same illusion.

## What &ldquo;what&rdquo; gets you that &ldquo;who&rdquo; doesn&rsquo;t

Asking &ldquo;who am I?&rdquo; produces identity. A fixed story. A narrative about yourself that you protect and defend and update reluctantly.

Asking &ldquo;what am I?&rdquo; produces something different: a map. A functional description of a dynamic system. One you can actually work with.

When I stopped trying to answer &ldquo;who&rdquo; and started answering &ldquo;what,&rdquo; my relationship with my own neurology changed. I stopped trying to fix the person and started <Link href="/blog/training-claude-neurological-patterns">engineering the system</Link>. What are the inputs that produce hyperfocus? What are the feedback loops that produce crashes? What external systems can I plug in to compensate for the executive function my brain doesn&rsquo;t generate reliably?

Claude became a node in my cognitive system. <Link href="/blog/building-always-on-ai-assistant">Hermes</Link> became a node in my publishing system. The Oura ring became a node in my physiological monitoring system. AirTags became nodes in the lost-item-recovery system that my ADHD brain requires.

None of those nodes changed &ldquo;who I am.&rdquo; They changed what systems I&rsquo;m part of. They changed the feedback loops. They changed the configuration. And the output &mdash; the behavior, the productivity, the quality of life &mdash; changed with it.

That&rsquo;s the whole insight. You don&rsquo;t fix a node by giving it a better story about itself. You fix a node by changing the system it operates in.

---

## The illusion is useful

I want to be careful here. Saying &ldquo;the self is an illusion&rdquo; sounds nihilistic. It&rsquo;s not.

The illusion of a stable self is incredibly useful. It lets you make plans. It lets you maintain relationships. It lets you tell a coherent story to the visa officer. It lets you get through the day without an existential crisis every time your neurotransmitter levels shift.

I&rsquo;m not arguing against the illusion. I&rsquo;m arguing that *knowing* it&rsquo;s an illusion gives you more room to move. When you stop defending a fixed identity and start treating yourself as a dynamic system, you can actually change things. You can swap out components. You can add feedback loops. You can build scaffolding. You can ask: what system would produce the behavior I want?

That question &mdash; not &ldquo;who am I?&rdquo; but &ldquo;what systems am I in, and what are they producing?&rdquo; &mdash; has been the most productive reframe of my life.

The LLMs didn&rsquo;t teach me this. My neurology taught me this. The LLMs just made it impossible to keep ignoring.
