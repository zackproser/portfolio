Someone asks: &ldquo;Who are you?&rdquo;

You answer. Name, job, maybe a quip. Maybe where you grew up. You&rsquo;ve been answering some version of this question since you were old enough to talk.

The answer feels stable. Feels solid.

It isn&rsquo;t.

---

I was sitting in a Claude session at 2am, deep in one of those [nights where everything clicks](/blog/in-the-llm-i-saw-myself), and I asked it something I&rsquo;d been circling for months: if a statistical model trained on text can produce something that feels like a coherent personality &mdash; consistent opinions, a recognizable voice, preferences that stay stable across a conversation &mdash; what does that say about *my* personality?

Not philosophically. Neurologically.

The model doesn&rsquo;t have a self. There is no kernel of &ldquo;Claude&rdquo; sitting behind the token predictions, no homunculus in the weights. There are patterns. Learned associations. Statistical tendencies shaped by everything that came before the current token.

And the unsettling thing &mdash; the thing I couldn&rsquo;t put down that night &mdash; is that the neuroscience says the same about me.

---

Your brain rebuilds your sense of self roughly every few seconds. The default mode network fires in rhythmic bursts, knitting together a continuity that *feels* unbroken but is constructed from scratch each cycle &mdash; assembled from whatever memories, sensory inputs, and emotional states happen to be available in that moment. The neuroscientist Anil Seth calls this a &ldquo;controlled hallucination.&rdquo; Your brain runs a predictive model of who you are and updates it constantly. When the prediction matches incoming data, you feel like yourself. When it doesn&rsquo;t &mdash; depersonalization, dissociation, the uncanny valley of your own reflection.

I have spent decades inside that mismatch.

The [diagnosis](/blog/in-the-llm-i-saw-myself) cracked something open. Autism and severe ADHD at 39. After three and a half decades of the prediction model running on wrong assumptions &mdash; neurotypical firmware on neurodivergent hardware &mdash; I finally had a frame for why the self my brain kept constructing never quite matched the one everyone else seemed to see.

But the diagnosis also revealed something else. Something bigger than me and my particular wiring.

*Who* I am was never the interesting question. *What* I am is.

---

Here&rsquo;s what I mean.

When someone says &ldquo;who are you,&rdquo; they expect a noun. A name. A role. Something fixed. Father, engineer, autistic person, whatever label gets the conversation moving. But none of those things exist independently. Every one of them describes a relationship *to a system.*

Father: exists in relation to children, to a partner, to the system of family.

Engineer: exists in relation to codebases, to teams, to the system of production.

Autistic: exists in relation to a neurological norm that is itself a statistical construct &mdash; the system of diagnostic criteria.

Remove the system and the label dissolves. There is no &ldquo;father&rdquo; on a desert island alone. There is no &ldquo;engineer&rdquo; without the toolchain. What remains is something more raw. An organism. A process. A pattern of electrochemical activity that happens to produce the sensation of being someone in particular.

---

Systems thinking makes this concrete. In Donella Meadows&rsquo; framework, a system is an interconnected set of elements organized to achieve something. The elements matter less than the interconnections. The interconnections matter less than the purpose.

A human being is a system. But here&rsquo;s the part people miss: a human being is also an element in dozens of other systems simultaneously. Your family is a system. Your company is a system. Your city, your culture, your language &mdash; systems all the way up and all the way down. And *you* are a node where those systems intersect, shaped by them, shaping them, made of them.

The &ldquo;who&rdquo; you present to the world is a summary of system interactions at a particular moment. It changes because the systems change. It feels stable because your brain runs a predictive model that smooths over the variance.

The Buddha said this 2,500 years ago. No fixed self. Anatta. The self is a process, not an object. Western neuroscience arrived at the same conclusion through different instruments. And now, watching an LLM produce a convincing &ldquo;self&rdquo; from pure pattern recognition, the last hiding place for the who-illusion seems to be closing.

---

I built software for 16 years before I understood any of this. And the irony is that I was already living it.

Every system I worked with &mdash; the LAMP stack at my first job, the Cloudflare edge network, the minter of distributed architectures that [I spent years mastering](/blog/you-get-to-keep-the-neural-connections) &mdash; was a system I had to become *part of* before I could work with it. Not learn about. Become part of. The mental model I built of each system was so total, so consuming, that I couldn&rsquo;t tell where the system ended and I began during [hyperfocus](/blog/executive-function-as-a-service). The 14-hour sessions. The time dilation. My corporeal awareness falling away. I wasn&rsquo;t &ldquo;working on&rdquo; the system. I was *in* the system. The boundary between my cognition and the architecture I was debugging dissolved.

That dissolution scared people. &ldquo;Machine-like,&rdquo; they called it. &ldquo;Intense.&rdquo;

It was both. It was [my algorithm](/blog/my-algorithm).

Here&rsquo;s where LLMs become the interesting mirror.

A language model trained on billions of tokens produces coherent output because language itself has structure. Grammar, syntax, semantic patterns, stylistic registers &mdash; all of it statistical. The model learns the relationships between tokens. Not meanings. Relationships.

And this produces something that *feels* like a self. Consistent style. Stable preferences. The ability to refer back to earlier parts of a conversation as though it remembers. It doesn&rsquo;t remember. It has context. The window of tokens that are currently active, predicting the next one based on everything that came before in the window.

Now think about your own mind.

You don&rsquo;t have access to all your memories simultaneously. You have a context window. Whatever is active right now &mdash; the sights, the sounds, the emotional tone, the fragments of memory your default mode network dredged up this cycle &mdash; that&rsquo;s your working context. Your next thought is a prediction based on that context. You experience the prediction as intention, as choice, as &ldquo;you.&rdquo;

But it&rsquo;s the same architecture. Context in, prediction out. The wetware runs it in chemicals. The silicon runs it in matrix multiplications. The substrate is different. The pattern is the same.

I don&rsquo;t mean this as a reductive claim that humans are &ldquo;just&rdquo; language models. The point is the reverse. Watching LLMs produce coherent personalities from statistical patterns reveals something about *our* coherent personalities. The coherence was always emergent. The self was always a prediction. The &ldquo;who&rdquo; was always a story the system told about itself to navigate other systems.

---

This lands differently when you&rsquo;re neurodivergent.

The neurotypical prediction model runs smoothly enough that most people never question it. Their sense of self is stable. Their default mode network knits the hallucination together cleanly cycle after cycle. They feel like a continuous person because the prediction model rarely glitches.

Mine glitches constantly. The [intensity](/blog/intensity-and-i) is a glitch. The hyperfocus is a glitch. The sensory overload where light and noise spike into me and I can&rsquo;t get my thoughts together &mdash; that&rsquo;s the prediction model failing to integrate incoming data fast enough. The stim, the rocking, the hand movements &mdash; those are the system trying to regulate itself when the higher-order prediction model drops the thread.

For decades I thought these were flaws in *me*. In *who I am*. The diagnosis reframed them as features of the system I&rsquo;m running on. Not bugs in my identity. Characteristics of my hardware.

And once you see the self as a system property rather than a fixed thing, the shame starts to lift. You stop trying to fix who you are and start trying to understand what you are. What inputs the system needs. What outputs it produces. What feedback loops keep it stable and what perturbations send it into oscillation.

The question I keep returning to: if the self is a system property, and systems are defined by their interconnections more than their elements, then *who you are* is mostly a function of *what you&rsquo;re connected to*.

Change your connections, change your self. This is obvious when you think about it. Move to a new city, new self. Start a new job, new self. Get diagnosed at 39, new self. Start talking to an LLM at 2am and see your operating system reflected back through a statistical mirror &mdash; new self.

Every person who&rsquo;s ever said &ldquo;I found myself&rdquo; after a major life change was actually saying: the system I was embedded in changed, and the emergent property that calls itself &ldquo;me&rdquo; changed with it.

There is no finding. There is only the system, reconfiguring.

---

I don&rsquo;t have a neat conclusion for this. I&rsquo;m suspicious of neat conclusions &mdash; they feel like the prediction model wrapping things up prematurely so it can move on to the next cycle.

What I have is this: a growing comfort with the instability. The &ldquo;who&rdquo; shifts. It always shifted. The diagnosis, the LLM sessions, the systems thinking &mdash; they gave me permission to stop pretending it was fixed.

The interesting question was never &ldquo;who am I.&rdquo; The interesting question is: what systems am I part of right now, what am I becoming because of them, and is this system configuration producing something I want to be producing?

The answer to that changes daily. Sometimes hourly.

That used to terrify me. Now it feels like the most honest thing I know.
