## The Documentation Load on Construction Managers

Let's be specific about what the documentation burden actually looks like on a commercial project. A typical day might require:

- **Daily construction report**: Weather, manpower counts by trade, work performed, deliveries received, equipment on site, issues and delays
- **Safety observations**: Pre-task plans, safety violations, near misses, corrective actions taken
- **RFIs**: Requests for information to design team on field conditions, drawing conflicts, specification questions
- **Change order support**: Documentation of scope changes, time impacts, cost basis
- **Subcontractor correspondence**: Direction letters, non-conformance notices, scheduling updates
- **Owner/GC communication**: Status updates, schedule implications, budget impacts
- **Meeting minutes**: Pre-construction, progress meetings, coordination meetings

That's not counting the actual construction management work — schedule updates, procurement, quality control, coordination. The documentation is overhead, but it's essential overhead. It protects you legally. It keeps the project running. It's the paper trail that wins disputes.

Most construction managers are doing this documentation in blocks — usually early morning before site walk or late evening from the hotel. They're reconstructing a full day from memory and a few iPhone notes. It works, but it's slow and the quality degrades over the course of a long project.

### Field-Generated RFIs

RFIs written from field observations — while standing at the issue location — are better RFIs than ones written from a desk. You catch more detail, you're more precise about location, you don't misremember the condition.

With WisprFlow, you can dictate an RFI immediately when you find the issue:

"RFI regarding structural drawing S-312, detail 4B, at column line D-7, Level 3. Field condition shows existing column does not align with new column grid as shown on drawings. Existing column centerline is approximately 3 inches north of new column grid. Need direction on how to handle connection. Provides photo reference 204-215. Dictated, pasted to your RFI template, photo attached, submitted. The whole thing takes three minutes instead of fifteen, and it's more accurate because you were looking at the problem when you wrote it.

### Change Order Documentation

Change order disputes are won and lost on documentation quality. The party with better contemporaneous records wins. WisprFlow helps you build that record in real time.

When you identify a scope change or a changed condition, document it immediately:

"Owner-directed change: concrete flatwork at south parking area, Lots C and D, now specified to be 5-inch slab with fiber reinforcement in lieu of 4-inch slab with wire mesh per original specification. Verbal direction received from owner representative John Harrington at 11:15 AM site meeting, April 27. Impact: approximately 800 additional cubic yards of concrete, labor for additional depth and fiber placement, schedule impact TBD pending revised submittals. Will follow up with written change order request by end of week."

That notation, timestamped and in your project file by noon, is worth far more than whatever you'd reconstruct from memory at 9 PM.
