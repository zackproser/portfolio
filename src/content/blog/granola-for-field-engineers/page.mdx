# Granola for Field Engineers: Document Site Visits Without Losing Technical Details

You're on a job site walking a client through a mechanical room while they describe a problem with their HVAC system. You're looking at equipment, asking follow-up questions, taking photos on your phone — and you're supposed to be taking notes at the same time. You can't do all of these things simultaneously. Something gets dropped, and it's usually the notes.

The alternative most field engineers default to: reconstruct from memory in the parking lot, or dictate voice memos on the drive back. Both methods lose detail. The specific measurements, the model numbers mentioned in passing, the sequence of events the client described — these compress or vanish under reconstruction.

Granola solves this by capturing the conversation while you focus on the work.

## What Field Engineers Actually Lose in Site Visits

The category of information most at risk isn't the big stuff — you'll remember the primary issue and the obvious action items. What disappears is the contextual technical detail:

**Exact specifications mentioned conversationally.** A client says "the unit was installed in 2019, it's the 150-ton Carrier unit on the east side, and we've been running it at higher capacity since the expansion." You heard it. You can paraphrase it. But the exact language — the specific year, the capacity callout, the timing relative to the expansion — compresses in reconstruction.

**The sequence of failure events.** "It started making noise in January, we had a tech out who said it was fine, then in March the pressure dropped and we noticed the bearing was loose." The timeline matters for diagnosis and liability. Getting it wrong creates problems.

**Decisions made during the walkthrough.** When a client says "just skip the east wing for now, that whole section is being demoed in six months," that's a documented scope decision. It should live somewhere that isn't your memory.

**Client concerns stated specifically.** "We can't have any downtime between 6 AM and 2 PM" is a constraint. "The last contractor left conduit runs that violate code, and we're worried about liability" is context that affects your approach. These specifics matter when you're writing your report or defending your recommendations.

## How Granola Works on a Site Visit Call

Most site visits involve a call component — either the initial client briefing happens on a video or phone call before you go out, or you're on a call during the visit with your office, or you're doing a remote walkthrough via video with a client on-site.

For any of these call scenarios, Granola runs on your Mac and captures the conversation. You join the call, Granola detects it and starts transcribing, and you focus entirely on the technical work. When the call ends, you get a searchable transcript and an AI-generated summary of what was discussed.

For field engineers, the practical workflow looks like this:

**Pre-visit call:** Client describes the issue over video. Granola captures their full description, including the technical details they mention in passing. You arrive on-site with an accurate record of what they told you, not your paraphrase.

**Remote inspection:** You're on video with a client walking you through the site. They're pointing the camera at equipment, reading off model numbers, describing symptoms. Granola gets all of it. Your hands are free to take notes on the structural or electrical issues you're observing — separate from what they're telling you.

**Post-visit debrief:** You call your office to describe what you found. Granola transcribes that conversation. Your verbal assessment becomes a searchable document that someone else can use to write the report or order parts.

## The Report Writing Problem

Field engineers write reports. Reports require specific details. The gap between site visit and report is where information loss compounds.

Without good documentation, the report-writing process involves:
- Reviewing photos and hoping they jog enough memory
- Checking voice memos if you made them (often you didn't)
- Reconstructing the client's description from the outline you wrote in the parking lot
- Calling the client back to confirm details you should have captured the first time

With Granola transcripts from the pre-visit call and any remote walkthrough calls, the report-writing process starts from a solid foundation. You have the client's exact words, the specific models and dates they mentioned, the constraints they stated. You're writing from documentation, not reconstruction.

## Inspection Calls and Remote Diagnostics

Remote diagnostics are increasingly common — a client on-site with a phone pointed at failing equipment, a field engineer on video walking them through the diagnosis. These calls generate a lot of technical information fast.

"Can you read me the nameplate on that unit?" leads to model numbers, serial numbers, electrical specs. The client reads them, you write them down, and transcription errors compound. Or you're focused on analyzing what you're seeing and tell yourself you'll write down the numbers in a minute — and then forget.

Granola captures the client reading the nameplate. The transcript shows exactly what they said. You can reference it when cross-checking specs or ordering parts without calling them back.

The same applies to inspection calls where multiple people are involved — a client, a contractor, possibly a code inspector. Multiple voices, multiple pieces of technical information, fast-moving conversation. Granola attributes speech to different speakers and captures the full exchange. You can go back and see exactly who said what and in what context.

## Scope Creep Documentation

Field engineering projects routinely expand beyond original scope during site visits. The client shows you something that wasn't in the original brief: "Oh, while you're here, can you look at this too?" You look. You offer a preliminary assessment. The client interprets that as a commitment.

Granola transcripts document these conversations as they actually happened. When the client later claims you said you'd handle the additional work for no extra charge, you have the transcript showing what was actually said. When the project manager asks why the scope changed, you have the conversation where it changed.

Having an accurate record means honest misunderstandings can be resolved with facts instead of competing memories.

## Practical Setup for Field Work

Granola is a Mac desktop app, which means it works best when site visits involve a call component from your laptop. For engineers who do a lot of in-person visits without any video call element, Granola covers the call-based portions of those workflows: the client briefing call before the visit, the office debrief after, and any remote diagnostic sessions.

For purely in-person work with no call component, Granola's core transcription feature doesn't apply directly. But many field engineering workflows include more call-based touchpoints than engineers initially think: project kickoffs, coordination calls with subcontractors, inspection walkthroughs via video, and client check-in calls throughout project execution.

Start by running Granola on all of those. The improvement in documentation quality for those calls creates compounding value — better notes going into each phase of the project, better documentation coming out.

For teams where multiple field engineers work the same project, Granola summaries can be shared directly. The engineer who did the initial site visit shares the Granola notes from that call. The next engineer who picks up the project reads the actual client conversation, not a summary of a summary.

The information loss that plagues field engineering documentation isn't inevitable. The conversations are happening. Granola makes sure they're captured.
