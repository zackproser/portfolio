## What we showed

Three production systems that the Applied AI team built and that the rest of WorkOS actually uses.

### Autonomous Agent Harness CLI

Give it a GitHub issue or a Linear ticket. It handles implementation, review, PR, and — this is the part — learns from corrections over time. We use it internally to orchestrate AI agent work across WorkOS open source projects. The interesting part isn't that an agent can write code; it's that the harness captures what went wrong on each run and feeds those corrections forward.

### GTM Intelligence Platform

Account enrichment and fit-scoring on demand. Our AEs query it from a dashboard, from Slack, or — and this is the part that surprised people — directly via MCP, so an agent can reach in and ask "is this account a fit, and why?" mid-conversation. The same scoring engine, three surfaces.

### Agentic Coding Platform (Horizon)

A code factory that turns engineering signals into shipped work. Horizon runs in secure cloud sandboxes, orchestrates implementation across Linear, GitHub, and Slack, and gets faster with every run. It surfaces its own friction, fixes it, and codifies what worked. The compounding behavior is the whole point.

## My segment: lessons from the trenches

I closed the showcase with what we actually learned shipping this much, this fast. A few things I hit on:

### Pick problems where the bar is "useful," not "impressive"

The systems that landed were the ones that replaced a workflow someone was already doing manually — fit-scoring an account, triaging an issue, drafting a PR. The ones that didn't land were the ones we built because they sounded cool in a roadmap doc. The bar is "did it take less time today than yesterday." Not "did it pass a benchmark."

### Build harnesses, not agents

The interesting unit of work isn't a prompt or a model — it's the harness around it: the scaffolding that gives the agent its tools, its constraints, its memory, and its recovery behavior. Every system we shipped got most of its leverage from harness work, not from the underlying model. Switch the model out, the harness keeps working.

### Evals are how you sleep at night

If you can't measure regressions, you can't ship into production. Every system we showed has an eval suite that runs against a fixed set of cases. When we changed prompts or models, the evals told us what got better and what got worse. Not "vibes."

### Constraints over instructions

This one carries over from the AIE London workshop, and it kept showing up here. Telling an agent "be careful" is unenforceable. Telling it "never make changes outside this directory" is enforceable. Every unconstrained dimension is a dimension the agent will drift on.

### Ship the rough thing, then polish

Most of these systems were ugly the first time we showed them. The Slack integration looked like a debug log. The dashboard was a single endpoint. We shipped them anyway, because the only way to know whether a thing is useful is to put it in front of people who would benefit. Polish came after adoption, not before it.

### The compounding loop is the whole game

The systems that compound — that get faster, smarter, or more useful with every use — are the ones worth investing in. A one-shot tool is a productivity boost. A compounding tool is a strategic asset. Horizon's "surfaces its own friction and codifies what worked" loop is the most important thing we shipped this year.

## Why we did this

We're at a moment where the gap between "AI is useful" and "AI is how this company works" is everything. The systems we showed are how WorkOS is closing that gap internally. The lessons we shared are what we'd tell another team trying to do the same thing.

If you're in that position — head of AI, CTO, or just an engineer trying to roll this out at your own org — the patterns transfer. The harness layer matters more than the model. The evals matter more than the demos. The constraints matter more than the instructions.
