# Granola for VC Partners: Founder Meeting Notes That Actually Capture the Signal

You did 14 founder meetings this week. By Friday, you can recall the details of three of them. The other eleven are compressed into a few fragments: "good team, interesting market, not quite there yet." That compression destroys the information that matters — the specific observations about team dynamics, the founder's edge, the market timing that made sense or didn't, the thing that bothered you about the answer to the traction question.

VC partners accumulate founder meetings faster than any professional accumulates equivalent informational density. A 45-minute meeting with a founder contains more signal-per-minute than almost any other professional interaction. And the standard toolset for capturing that signal — your memory, a CRM note typed between calls, a bulleted list of key points — keeps almost none of it.

## Why Current Note-Taking Fails VC Partners

The core problem isn't that partners don't take notes. It's that the notes they take don't capture the things that actually inform decisions.

A typical note from a founder meeting: "Team: two engineers + one BD person. Pre-revenue. Want $2M on $20M post-money. Good energy." That's useful for jogging memory in the next 48 hours. It's worthless for IC meeting prep two weeks later, for pattern matching across dozens of companies in the same vertical, or for reviewing your own judgment six months later.

What actually matters from a founder meeting:

**The founder's framing of the problem.** Did they describe the problem as they see it, or as they think investors want to hear it? Did their framing match yours? Did they acknowledge uncertainty or project unearned confidence?

**The answers to hard questions.** When you asked "what happens if a competitor with more funding enters this space?" — what did they say? Not the polished answer. The one that revealed how they actually think about competitive moats.

**Tension signals.** The moments where the two co-founders contradicted each other, where the emotional dynamics showed through the pitch structure, where you saw the gap between what they said and what the data indicated.

**Specific metrics with context.** "We've grown 3x in six months" means nothing without the base, the segment, and the trajectory context. The specific numbers and the story behind them — that's what makes this different from the last ten pitches you heard.

## How Granola Works for VC Partner Workflows

Granola runs as a Mac app. When you join a founder video call, Granola detects the meeting and starts transcribing automatically. The founder doesn't need to install anything — you're the only one running it.

After the meeting, Granola generates a structured AI summary that surfaces decisions, action items, and key discussion points. You can add sparse notes in Granola's notepad during the call — just anchors like "Series B pricing red flag" or "co-founder tension on technical co-lead question" — and those get integrated into the summary.

The practical workflow:

**Morning meeting block.** Four back-to-back founder calls. You run Granola on all of them. Between calls you might add a quick note or two, but mostly you're focusing on the conversation.

**Between-meeting break.** You open Granola and review the summaries. Each one has the key information extracted: team composition, ask details, market sizing as discussed, traction specifics. You add your own evaluation notes while the meetings are fresh.

**IC meeting prep.** Before the committee meeting, you pull up the Granola summaries for the deals being discussed. You have the founders' exact words on key questions, your real-time observations, and the specific metrics they shared. You're not relying on a CRM note that says "good team, interesting market."

## Pattern Recognition Across Founders

VC partners develop pattern recognition by seeing many companies. The value of pattern recognition depends on the quality of observations accumulated over time.

The founder who says "our moat is the data we're collecting" is saying something specific. The founder who says "our moat is the data we're collecting from 10,000 users in the dental vertical, where switching costs are real because we're embedded in their billing workflow" is saying something different. The first statement is a pitch cliché. The second is a defensible claim with specifics you can evaluate.

Without Granola, these statements compress in memory. After 20 meetings, "data moat" becomes a generic signal instead of a specific claim that varies in quality. With Granola transcripts, you can search for every founder who claimed a data moat and compare the specifics of each claim. Pattern recognition gets fed with data, not compressed memory.

This works across sectors too. When you're evaluating three companies in the fintech space at the same time, being able to pull up the exact discussion of regulatory risk from each founder call — not your summary of each discussion, the actual conversation — gives you a much better basis for comparison.

## IC Meeting Preparation

Investment committee meetings are where deals get approved or killed. Preparation quality directly affects outcomes.

The typical IC prep: review the CRM entry, read the partner's investment memo, skim the notes from partner meetings. The notes are thin because partners meet with too many founders to write detailed notes after each one.

A better IC prep: review the CRM entry, read the investment memo, and pull up the Granola summaries from every meeting you had with the founder and their team. The Granola transcript shows you how the founder described the traction in the first meeting versus the third meeting, which specific objections they handled well, and what signals they gave about pivots they're already considering.

The transcript captures temporal patterns: how the team's confidence shifted between the first meeting and the second, how the metrics evolved across the diligence process, how the founder handled tough questions on follow-up calls versus the polished pitch meeting. These temporal patterns are the strongest signals for founder quality, and they're exactly the information that gets lost in summarized notes.

## Portfolio Company Check-Ins

After writing the check, VC partners have ongoing governance and advisory interactions with portfolio companies: board calls, board prep calls, ad-hoc check-ins with founders, and deep dives on operational challenges.

These conversations contain decisions and context that matter later:

"Based on this month's burn rate and runway projection, we agreed not to pursue the second hire this quarter and revisit in Q3."

"The founder disclosed that the enterprise client churn spike is being handled with a price restructure and is confident it's a quarter-to-quarter normalization, not a trend."

"We discussed the potential acquisition offer and decided together that the timing was wrong because the founder wants to reach $5M ARR first to command a better multiple."

Each of these conversations has implications for the fund's position, for follow-on investment decisions, and for the board's understanding of operational risk. The agreement from each meeting should be documented with enough specificity that it doesn't get reframed when it becomes inconvenient.

Granola transcripts of board prep calls and portfolio check-ins create that documentation without requiring either side to write formal meeting minutes.

## Competitive Intelligence and Deal Flow Quality

Over time, the Granola archive becomes a competitive intelligence asset. Search across hundreds of founder meetings and you can:

- Track how many founders are pitching in the same space, and how their framings differ
- Identify which trends are saturating (too many founders making the same pitch with the same thesis)
- Recall which founders you met early who later became relevant competitors in other markets
- Compare the market analysis provided by different founders to triangulate which version of the market thesis is most accurate

The search functionality across meeting transcripts is the feature that matters most for the compounding value play. A single meeting transcript is useful. A searchable archive of 500 meetings is a proprietary dataset of founder intelligence that no one else has access to.

## Practical Notes

Granola is a Mac app. It works on video calls — Zoom, Google Meet, and other standard video platforms. For in-person meetings, Granola's core transcription feature doesn't apply, though the sparse note-taking functionality in the notepad still works as a scratchpad.

For partners who do both in-person and video founder meetings, run Granola on every video call. The archive builds itself. For in-person meetings, keep using your existing manual process and fold those notes into the same workflow.

No one on the founder side needs to know about or install Granola. It runs on your machine, transcribes the audio, and the output is your property.
