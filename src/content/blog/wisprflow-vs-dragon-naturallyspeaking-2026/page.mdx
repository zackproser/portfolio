I've used Dragon NaturallySpeaking on and off for over a decade. I've also been using WisprFlow for about eight months. These are not the same product, and they're not aimed at the same user — but they compete for the same wallet.

Here's what I found spending a month going back to Dragon after years on WisprFlow.

## What Dragon Does Well

Dragon is the professional transcription tool that lawyers, doctors, and court reporters have relied on since the late 1990s. When it works, it works extremely well.

Voice commands are thorough. You can navigate entire applications, format documents, and control your desktop without touching a keyboard. Dragon knows about hundreds of third-party applications and ships with voice command sets for Microsoft Office, browsers, and more.

Dictation accuracy with a trained profile is genuinely impressive. After 15-30 minutes of reading calibration text, Dragon learns your voice and produces remarkably clean transcriptions. Medical and legal terminology vocabularies are available as add-ons.

## Where Dragon Falls Short

Dragon is a desktop application. It runs on Windows, and the Mac version — Dragon for Mac — has been discontinued. If you're on macOS, you're running Windows in a VM or on separate hardware. That's a non-trivial overhead to add to your workflow.

The install process alone is a journey. You're downloading a multi-hundred-megabyte installer, running through wizard screens, creating user profiles, and training the software before you get to actual work.

You open it, you speak, you get text. The text appears in whatever application you're currently focused on, whether that's Notes, Slack, Obsidian, a code editor, or your email client. There's no application-specific training required.
