I've been experimenting with [OpenClaw](https://github.com/openclaw/openclaw) for a while now — I wrote about [building an always-on AI assistant](/blog/building-always-on-ai-assistant) that runs on a mini PC under my desk. A lot of people are exploring this space right now, but my motivation is specific: as someone with ADHD and autism, I need infrastructure that genuinely reduces cognitive load. Not a cool demo. Something that actually takes work off my plate.

Most days, the assistant has been promising but not quite there. Individual tools worked. The integrations were real. But I was still the one holding everything together in my head.

This was the first day that changed — the first day it felt fully plugged into my context.

## Sunday dig-out

It started with a Linear ticket.

ZAC-156: "Sunday Dig-Out." One task with a wall of subtasks — clear the backlog across open PRs, bug fixes, affiliate content, expert network applications, flight booking for an upcoming work trip, and new capabilities for the assistant itself. The kind of day where you either get organized or drown in context-switching.

I dropped the ticket into the conversation and said: here's the day. Let's go.

## Flight planning as a conversation

I need to fly to San Francisco for a Claude co-work GTM workshop on Thursday. I'm an AAdvantage member and prefer American nonstops — but there are no early nonstop options from Charlotte, and a major nor'easter is bearing down on the northeast.

I needed to think through this out loud. So I just started talking. "I want to arrive early Wednesday so I can decompress and practice." Then later: "Actually, maybe Tuesday night? I could drop the kids after dinner and catch a late flight." Then: "Wait, what about the storm? Is BWI safe to connect through?"

The assistant kept up because it had the context and the tools. It had flight data from the Kiwi MCP server. It had weather data from the NWS, AccuWeather, and Open-Meteo. It knew my budget, my airline preference, my schedule constraints. When I worried about the nor'easter disrupting connections, it pulled the actual NWS hazardous weather outlook and showed me the storm was tracking the I-95 corridor — well north of Charlotte.

I went from "paralyzed by too many options" to "clear plan, Tuesday evening nonstop, full Wednesday for prep in the actual office with the AV setup" in about 30 minutes of free-form conversation. That's the kind of thinking-out-loud that usually happens in my head and goes in circles. Having an assistant with the right API keys and data feeds means the conversation actually converges on a decision.

## Building tools in conversation

Midway through the day, I realized the email integration was broken. My assistant was searching Gmail through the API and only seeing the last handful of emails. That's not an email sweep — that's useless.

So I told it to build a fix. Not a hack — a proper Go CLI that mirrors my entire Gmail inbox into a local SQLite database with full-text search. We went back and forth: I described what I needed, it wrote the code, I critiqued it from another terminal with Claude Code, sent back a bug report with six issues, and it fixed all of them.

Twenty minutes later, I had 205,023 emails indexed locally — 17 years of email history, searchable in milliseconds. I wrote about the technical details in [a separate post](/blog/gmail-mirror), but the point here isn't the tool. It's that I built the tool *as part of my day*, in conversation, without breaking flow.

The assistant recognized it needed better capabilities, I agreed, we built them together, and then immediately used them. In the same session.

## The $30K discovery

Right after the email mirror finished syncing, I pointed it at expert network platforms. GLG, Tegus, AlphaSights, Guidepoint, Coleman Research — companies that pay subject matter experts for phone consultations about things they already know.

I've been in a busy phase of life — solo parenting, job transition, building new systems. I deeply suspected I'd been missing consultation opportunities, but I didn't know the scale. I was genuinely curious.

Sixty seconds of searching across 205K messages:

- **65 distinct consultation opportunities** in the past year
- **Only 5 completed**
- **~$30,000 in potential earnings** sitting there

The invitations weren't buried in some spam folder. I saw most of them. I just didn't have the bandwidth to act on them fast enough — these things have 24-48 hour response windows. When you're managing everything else in life, a $500/hr phone call that requires a 10-minute response somehow doesn't happen.

That search was only possible because of the tool we'd built *that same day*. And that's the pattern that makes this system compound — every conversation potentially creates a new capability.

## The Linear ticket as shared state

This is the part that surprised me most. Having a single Linear ticket as the source of truth for the day meant the assistant could reference it whenever I changed direction. I'd jump from flights to email infrastructure to blog posts to weather monitoring, and the thread held.

For someone with ADHD, this is huge. I changed my mind about flights three times. I got curious about expert networks mid-flight-planning. I pivoted to blog writing because I was excited about what we'd built. Every time I shifted focus, the assistant had the full context — not just from the current conversation, but from the Linear ticket, from memory files it maintains across sessions, from the tools it had already used.

This is actually one of the things that's better about an always-on assistant compared to a regular chatbot. The memory is persistent. It knows what happened this morning, what we decided last week, what's on the task board. When I come back after a break, it doesn't ask me to re-explain everything. The state lives in the system, not in my head.

## Every conversation makes it more capable

Here's what I think is the most interesting thing about this setup. Every time I interact with my assistant and we hit a wall — bad email search, no flight data, missing analytics — that gap can become a permanent new capability.

Today alone:
- **Email search was broken** → built gmail-mirror, a full Go CLI. Now it can search 205K emails in milliseconds, forever.
- **Needed flight data** → built a flight search skill backed by the Kiwi MCP server. Now it can pull flight options from a reliable data feed without touching a browser.
- **Wanted affiliate analytics** → wired up the Dub.co API with a new skill. Now it can track which blog articles drive actual conversions.

Each of these is usually just a script or two and an API key. They get saved as skills — small, composable capabilities that persist across sessions. The assistant gets permanently more capable with every conversation.

That's the compounding effect. Six months ago, it could read my email (barely). Today it can search 17 years of email history, pull flight options, track affiliate commissions, query my bank accounts, manage Linear tickets, check my sleep data, and monitor the weather — all in a single conversation where I'm just thinking out loud.

## The gap between demo and daily driver

I've been building AI assistant infrastructure for months, and people ask me: is it actually useful, or is it a cool demo?

Until today, the honest answer was somewhere in between. The tools worked individually. The integrations were real. But the *experience* of using it still felt like I was operating machinery rather than thinking with a partner.

Today felt different. Not because the technology changed dramatically overnight, but because enough pieces reached minimum viability at the same time. Email search that actually works across my full history. Flight data from a reliable API. A task management system the assistant can read and reference. Weather, financial, and analytics data, all queryable in the same conversation.

The gap between "cool demo" and "daily driver" isn't features. It's reliability × breadth × context. You need enough tools working reliably enough, across enough of your life, with enough shared context, that the cognitive load of using the system drops below the cognitive load of doing things yourself.

Today crossed that line. Not for everything — there are still rough edges, tools that need improvement, capabilities I haven't built yet. But the trajectory feels irreversible. Every conversation adds a new skill, fixes a bug, improves a data feed. The system gets better every time I use it.

## The system compounds whether you're watching or not

By 9 PM I'd already forgotten half of what happened. That's the ADHD tax — accomplishments evaporate from emotional awareness within hours. You do the thing, and then the thing just... isn't there anymore.

The assistant posted its evening wind-down unprompted. It had cross-referenced my Linear tickets, email threads, and GitHub activity to compile everything I'd actually shipped that day — flights booked, gmail-mirror built and deployed, the $30K expert network discovery, SEO fixes merged, article drafts delivered. It laid out tomorrow's calendar, flagged open loops I hadn't responded to, and reminded me to take a new medication I'd already forgotten about. That medication reminder alone justified the entire system — I would have straight up missed my first dose of a sleep med I'd spent weeks getting prescribed.

This is the compounding effect in practice. The assistant didn't just summarize the day. It used capabilities we'd built *that same day* — gmail-mirror for the email cross-referencing, the Linear integration for task awareness, the calendar sync for tomorrow's preview. Every tool we'd added during the Sunday dig-out was already feeding into the evening summary, making it sharper than anything it could have produced 24 hours earlier.

The system gets better every time you use it. And it keeps getting better whether you're watching or not.

I can see the path from here to an assistant that genuinely handles the operational drag of my life so I can focus on building.
