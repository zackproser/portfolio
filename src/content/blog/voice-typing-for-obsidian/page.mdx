## Why Voice Journaling Is Different

**Typing journals are edited thoughts.** When you type, your internal editor kicks in. You rephrase, delete, restructure before the words hit the page. The raw thought — the actual insight — gets polished away.

**Voice journals are raw thoughts.** When you talk, you think out loud. The ideas come faster than your editor can filter them. You capture connections, emotions, and half-formed insights that never survive the typing process.

**WisprFlow makes them readable.** The AI cleanup bridges the gap. You speak naturally — with pauses, filler words, tangents — and what appears in Obsidian is clean, readable text. Not a raw transcript, but not over-edited either.

## My Daily Workflow

**Morning brain dump (5 minutes).** Before I check email or Slack, I open today's daily note and dictate everything on my mind. Worries, ideas, priorities, random thoughts. No structure, just talking. WisprFlow turns it into readable paragraphs.

**Post-meeting capture (2 minutes).** Right after a meeting ends, I dictate my takeaways, action items, and things I want to remember. This captures details I'd forget by the time I got around to typing notes.

**Evening reflection (3 minutes).** What went well, what didn't, what I'm thinking about. Linking to other notes happens naturally when I mention projects or people — I add the `[[wiki-links]]` after dictating.

## Tips for Obsidian + Voice

- **Dictate first, link later.** Get the content out by voice, then spend 30 seconds adding `[[wiki-links]]`, tags, and formatting.
- **Use daily notes as the inbox.** Dictate everything into today's daily note. Process and file during your weekly review.
- **Templates still work.** Create your journal template with headers, then dictate under each section.
- **Don't try to be perfect.** The whole point is capturing raw thinking. WisprFlow cleans up the language; you don't need to speak in perfect prose.
