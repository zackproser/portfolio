# Granola for Engineering Managers: Stop Losing 1:1 Context Across 20 Direct Reports

Engineering managers live in meetings. 1:1s, sprint planning, architecture reviews, incident retrospectives, cross-functional syncs, skip-levels, performance conversations. A solid EM with a team of 8-12 people is in structured conversations for 3-4 hours every day.

The problem with that much meeting volume: context evaporates. What did you discuss with Sarah about her career growth three 1:1s ago? What was the blocker David mentioned that you said you'd follow up on? What did the retrospective surface about the CI/CD pipeline that someone was supposed to investigate?

You don't remember. Not because you're bad at your job, but because you're running at capacity and your brain can't cache 20 people's worth of ongoing context across 40+ conversations per month.

## The 1:1 Context Problem

Good 1:1s build on previous 1:1s. You reference what someone said last month, follow up on the thing they were worried about, connect the dots between what they're experiencing and the team dynamics you're tracking. That continuity is what separates an EM who actually develops people from one who just shows up weekly with a list of agenda items.

Building that continuity from memory across 10+ direct reports is cognitively impossible at scale. The standard workaround — a shared doc that both parties update — requires discipline that most people don't sustain past the second month.

Granola gives you automatic continuity. Every 1:1 is captured and summarized. Before next week's 1:1, you pull up last week's Granola notes. You see exactly what was discussed, what you committed to, what your report said was stressing them out. The conversation picks up with real context instead of a fresh start.

## Sprint Planning and Architecture Reviews

High-information technical meetings are where meeting notes matter most and are hardest to capture well. Sprint planning surfaces velocity data, dependency concerns, scope questions, and team capacity issues — all in a 45-minute window. Architecture reviews generate design decisions, tradeoffs discussed, and constraints that influenced the final approach.

Someone typing notes in real time can't keep up with the technical content while also participating in the decisions. Someone trying to reconstruct architecture decisions after the fact misses the reasoning that led to them.

Granola captures the technical content accurately — including the specifics of what was decided and why — so the decision record reflects reality. Three months later when someone asks why the team chose that database partitioning strategy, the answer is in Granola, not in a half-remembered summary document.

## Incident Retrospectives

Retros are the highest-value meeting most engineering teams run and the one with the worst follow-through on action items. Great insight surfaces in the room, everyone nods, someone makes a note in a doc — and 80% of the identified improvements never happen because there's no accountability structure.

Granola captures the retrospective and extracts action items with owners and context. Instead of a flat bullet list of action items, you have a summary that includes the discussion that led to each item — which means the person responsible understands why it matters, not just what they're supposed to do.

## Performance and Career Conversations

Performance conversations need accurate records. When you're in a year-end review discussing someone's growth trajectory or documenting a performance concern, accuracy matters. The notes from those conversations can be referenced months later in promotion discussions, PIP conversations, or — in difficult situations — HR proceedings.

Relying on post-meeting recollection for performance conversations introduces risk. Granola gives you an accurate record of what was discussed, what commitments were made, and what the employee said about their own goals and concerns.

## The Cross-Functional Sync Problem

EMs spend meaningful time in cross-functional meetings — product/engineering alignment, platform team coordination, incident command, executive business reviews. These meetings involve stakeholders who aren't on your team and decisions that affect dependencies your team cares about.

Granola captures these sessions the same way it captures 1:1s. When your product lead says in a planning sync that the launch date is firm and no scope reduction is possible, that's in your Granola notes. When you need to escalate a capacity issue, you have timestamped context for the conversation where the constraints were set.
