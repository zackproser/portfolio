# The All-Apple House: Building a Smart Home to Survive Solo Parenting Two Toddlers

I have two toddlers, no partner in the house, and a job that requires deep focus. The math on that doesn't work unless the house itself helps. So I made the house help.

Over the past year I've wired the entire place with Aqara cameras, Apple TVs, smart plugs, and Siri automations — all funneled through Apple's Home app. The goal was a single ecosystem where every device talks to every other device and I can monitor, control, and automate everything from my watch, my phone, my laptop, or my Vision Pro.

The hardware side worked. The software side is a disaster. More on that below.

## Why All-Apple

I didn't start with a thesis about ecosystem lock-in. I started with a problem: I need to see my kids at all times, from any device, with zero friction.

That means the cameras need to work with the Home app. The Home app needs to work on my iPhone, my Apple Watch, my MacBook, and my Vision Pro. The Apple TVs need to respond to Siri. The smart plugs need to show up in Home so I can schedule them. Every device I add needs to slot into the same control plane.

Apple's HomeKit is that control plane. Everything routes through it. One app, one ecosystem, one authentication layer. When it works, I say "Hey Siri, show me the nursery" on my watch and get a live camera feed in two seconds. When it doesn't work — which is most of the time — I want to throw the watch into the yard.

I chose the lock-in deliberately. The alternative was stitching together four different apps, three different cloud backends, and a home automation server I'd need to maintain. I have two toddlers. I don't have time to run a Kubernetes cluster for my light switches.

## Indoor Cameras: Aqara G3s Everywhere

Four Aqara G3 cameras cover the interior of the house:

- **Living room** — mounted high in the corner, angled to see the whole play area and the couch
- **Kitchen** — on top of the fridge, pointed at the counter and the floor where the kids eat
- **Kid's room #1** — on the dresser, covering the crib and the door
- **Kid's room #2** — same setup, opposite room

The G3 is a pan-and-tilt camera with HomeKit Secure Video support. That means the video streams are end-to-end encrypted through iCloud and show up natively in the Home app on every Apple device. No third-party app required for viewing. No separate cloud account. The camera is a HomeKit accessory, and that's all it needs to be.

The pan-and-tilt matters for the kids' rooms. When one of them is moving around the crib at 2 AM, I can rotate the camera from my phone without getting out of bed to see what they're doing. Are they standing up? Did they throw the blanket out? Are they actually crying or just sleep-babbling? The camera answers those questions without me walking down the hall and risking waking the other one.

## Outdoor Cameras: Aqara G5 Wi-Fi on the Fence

Two Aqara G5 Wi-Fi cameras are mounted directly to the wooden fence in the backyard. One covers the left side — the swing set and the sandbox. The other covers the right side — the patio and the back door.

I bolted them straight into the fence posts with lag screws. No fancy mounting brackets. The G5 is weather-rated, and the fence gives them height and angle without needing to drill into the house.

The night vision on these cameras is absurd. It always looks like daytime. I've pulled up the backyard feed at 11 PM and had to check the clock because the image was so bright and clear. IR LEDs plus a decent sensor means the raccoons raiding the trash cans at 3 AM show up in vivid detail. More importantly, it means I can see the backyard clearly if the kids are playing outside and I need to step inside for thirty seconds to grab something.

## Doorbell Cameras: Front and Side Coverage

Two Aqara doorbell cameras face out the front door. Together they cover the entire front of the house — the porch, the driveway, the walkway from the street. A third doorbell camera watches the side door, covering my car and the path anyone would take to reach the kitchen entrance.

The doorbell cameras serve a different function than the indoor and backyard cameras. Those are for watching the kids. The doorbell cameras are for knowing who's at the house. Package deliveries, visitors, the occasional person I don't recognize walking up the driveway — I see all of it as a notification on my watch before they ring the bell. That matters when both kids are napping and the doorbell would wake them.

## The Monitoring Setup

Here's how the pieces fit together in practice.

**During the day**, the kids are usually in the living room or the kitchen. I pull up the Home app on my MacBook while I'm working and leave it in a small window. One tap switches between cameras. If I'm in a meeting and hear a crash from downstairs, I can glance at the feed without leaving my desk.

**When they're playing outside**, I switch to the backyard cameras. Same Home app, different room. The feeds are fast enough that I can step inside to get water or use the bathroom and still see exactly what they're doing.

**At night**, I put my phone on the nightstand to charge and leave the Home app open to one of the kids' room cameras. The audio comes through the phone speaker. If either kid starts crying or calling out, I hear it immediately. This replaced a standalone baby monitor and works better — the video is higher quality, the audio is clearer, and I don't need a separate device on the nightstand.

**On my Apple Watch**, I can ask Siri to show me any camera at any time. "Hey Siri, show me the living room." The feed pops up on the watch face. It's tiny, but it's enough to see whether the kids are where they should be. I use this most when I'm in another room and don't want to pull out my phone.

**On my Vision Pro**, I can pull up camera feeds while watching TV. If I'm watching something after the kids are in bed and hear a noise from the monitor, I ask Siri to show me the nursery and the feed appears as a floating window next to whatever I'm watching. I wrote about the Vision Pro multitasking setup in detail in [How to Play Steam Link on Vision Pro While Watching TV](/blog/play-steam-link-vision-pro-while-watching-tv) — the same spatial computing that lets me game and watch TV simultaneously lets me monitor the kids' rooms without interrupting anything.

## Apple TV and Siri: Hands-Free When It Works

I have an Apple TV in the downstairs living room and another in my bedroom. Both are HomeKit hubs, which means they also serve as the local processing backbone for the camera network.

The real value of the Apple TVs is hands-free control via Siri. The use case that matters most: I'm changing a diaper, both hands occupied, and I need entertainment going for the other toddler. "Hey Siri, turn on the TV and play Spidey and His Amazing Friends." Or: "Hey Siri, turn on the TV and put on YouTube." No remote. No phone. No stopping what I'm doing.

At the end of the night, when the kids are finally asleep and I collapse onto the couch: "Hey Siri, turn off the TV." Done. On the best days, the whole evening sequence — dinner, bath, books, bed — runs with voice commands handling the media and lights while my hands handle the kids.

## Smart Plugs and Automations

I have a white noise water fountain in my bedroom. It helps me sleep. Running it 24 hours a day wastes electricity and burns out the pump. So I put it on a smart plug and automated it through the Home app.

Every night at 10 PM, the fountain turns on. Every morning at 7 AM, it turns off. I never touch it. The Home app handles the schedule. The plug cost $15 and saved me from one more thing to remember.

I did the same thing with two bug zappers outside. They're on smart plugs with evening-to-morning schedules. The bugs get zapped when it matters. The zappers shut off during the day when nobody's outside and the bugs aren't active.

The automation layer in the Home app is basic but functional for timed schedules. Set a time, pick an action, choose which days. For the things I've automated so far — fountain, bug zappers — that's all I need.

## Siri Is Infuriating Garbage (May 2026)

I need to be direct about this because the pitch above sounds great and the reality is miserable.

Siri fails about 90% of the time. I am not exaggerating. The failure modes are varied and maddening:

- **"I'm having trouble connecting to the network."** You're on Wi-Fi. You're three feet from the router. Everything else works.
- **"I couldn't find that device."** The device is right there in the Home app. It shows up in the device list. Siri can't find it.
- **"Something went wrong. Please try again."** I try again. Same result. I try again. Same result. I try again. The TV turns on. I have no idea what changed.
- **Long pauses followed by nothing.** Siri acknowledges the request, the spinning indicator appears, ten seconds pass, and then nothing happens. No error. No feedback. The request vanished.
- **Wrong device.** "Turn on the living room TV" turns on the bedroom TV. Or turns on a light. Or does nothing.

The worst part is the inconsistency. The same command that worked yesterday at 8 PM fails today at 8 PM. The same camera that pulled up instantly on the watch three hours ago now gives me "I'm having trouble with that." Nothing changed. Nothing was updated. The network is the same. The devices are the same. Siri decides, arbitrarily, not to work.

When I'm alone with two toddlers, one screaming and one climbing something, and I ask Siri to show me a camera feed — and Siri fails — that failure cascades. I now need to find my phone, unlock it, open the Home app, find the room, tap the camera, and wait for the feed to load. That's thirty seconds I don't have. The whole point of the voice interface was to eliminate those thirty seconds. Siri makes the promise and breaks it nine times out of ten.

Apple, if anyone there reads this: fix Siri. The hardware ecosystem you've built is genuinely good. The cameras work. The HomeKit protocol works. The Home app is fine. The Apple TVs are solid. The watch integration is clever. All of it is undermined by a voice assistant that cannot reliably execute basic commands. This is the weakest link in the entire Apple ecosystem, and it has been for years. The hardware earns trust. Siri destroys it.

## What's Next: Planned Upgrades

The foundation is in place. The cameras, the Apple TVs, the smart plugs, the automations — the hardware layer works. The next phase is expanding what the house can do on its own.

**Govee indoor lights.** I bought Govee smart light strips and bulbs for every room. Once they're wired up and added to HomeKit, I'll be able to say "Turn on the lights" or "Turn off the lights" per room. Right now, walking through the house at night with a toddler on my hip means fumbling for light switches in the dark. Voice-controlled lighting in every room eliminates that.

**Motion sensors.** I bought Aqara motion sensors but haven't installed them yet. The plan: mount them in the hallway, the stairway, and the kitchen. At night, when someone walks through, the lights turn on automatically. No voice command needed, no switch to find. The sensor triggers the light, the light turns off after a timeout. The house responds to presence instead of waiting for instructions.

**Better automations.** Right now my automations are simple time-based schedules. The next step is condition-based automations — if motion is detected in the hallway after 9 PM, turn on the hallway light at 20% brightness. If no motion for five minutes, turn it off. If the front door camera detects a person, send a notification and turn on the porch light. The Home app supports these triggers. I haven't built them yet because the motion sensors aren't mounted.

**Timer-based routines.** Bedtime routine for the kids follows the same sequence every night. Bath, pajamas, books, bed. I want to automate the environmental side of that sequence: at 7:30 PM, dim the kids' room lights to 30%, turn on the white noise machine, and set the thermostat down two degrees. One automation that runs the same way every night, so I don't have to remember any of it.

## The Honest Assessment

The hardware is right. Aqara cameras are reliable, the video quality is good, the HomeKit integration works, and the night vision on the G5 outdoor cameras is exceptional. The Apple TVs serve double duty as entertainment devices and HomeKit hubs. The smart plugs do exactly what they need to do. Every physical device I've installed has performed as expected.

The software is the bottleneck. Siri is unreliable to the point of being a liability. The Home app occasionally forgets devices exist and needs to be force-quit and reopened. HomeKit Secure Video sometimes takes 15 seconds to load a feed that should be instant. Automations occasionally don't fire, with no error message and no log to check.

I chose the Apple ecosystem because the integration promise was the strongest. One company, one platform, one app that controls everything. That promise holds at the hardware and protocol level. It collapses at the software level. The devices are talking to each other correctly. The software layer that's supposed to make them accessible to a human — especially a human whose hands are full of toddlers — is the part that fails.

I got the right hardware in place. I'm waiting for the software to catch up. Apple needs to reinvest serious engineering time in getting Siri and the Home app to be as reliable as the devices they control. The hardware team shipped a good product. The software team needs to match them.

In the meantime, I'm glad the cameras work, the automations work, and the bug zappers turn off by themselves. That's enough to make the house help. It could help a lot more if Siri would do her job.
