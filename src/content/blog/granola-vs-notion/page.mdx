# Granola vs Notion: Different Tools, Different Jobs

I see this comparison come up a lot: Granola versus Notion. People want to know which one they should use, or whether they need both. The honest answer is that these are fundamentally different tools that solve different problems, but they do overlap in one specific area — meeting notes — which is where the confusion comes from.

Granola is an AI meeting notes tool. It connects to Google Meet, Zoom, and Teams, captures audio from your device, and produces structured summaries with decisions and action items. Notion is a workspace and database system that can hold meeting notes if you put them there. That's the comparison people are actually making, and it's a bit like comparing a professional recording studio to Google Drive.

Let me break down how each actually works.

## How Granola Works

Granola takes a specific, narrow approach to productivity. You connect it to your calendar and your video conferencing tools. When a meeting starts, Granola captures the audio directly from your device. After the meeting ends, you get a structured summary: key points discussed, decisions made, action items assigned, and follow-up notes.

The formatting happens automatically. You don't structure anything manually. The AI parses what was said and outputs something immediately useful — a summary you can send to attendees, paste into a Slack channel, or forward to someone who couldn't make it.

The integrations matter here. Granola pushes its output directly into Notion, into your CRM, into Slack. For team environments, this means meeting summaries appear exactly where work actually happens without you manually exporting and copying anything.

The Notion integration deserves specific mention because it's the reason this comparison exists. Granola can create a new page in Notion for each meeting, populate it with the summary, and tag it appropriately. If you're already a Notion user, this works well. If you're not, Granola's output still stands on its own — you get a clean, structured document without needing to put it anywhere.

## How Notion Works

Notion is a modular workspace. You can build databases, wikis, project trackers, documentation systems, and yes — meeting notes. But unlike Granola, Notion doesn't do anything automatically. You create the structure. You decide what goes where. You maintain the consistency.

For meeting notes specifically, Notion gives you a blank canvas and says "make it useful." You can create a template. You can build a database of meetings with properties for date, attendees, and status. You can link meeting notes to projects, tasks, and people. But all of that structuring is your job.

The strength of Notion is that when you've built the structure, everything connects. A meeting note linked to a project shows up in that project's context. Action items from meetings can flow into task databases. The interconnectedness is powerful — if you invest in setting it up properly.

The weakness is the same as the strength. If you don't consistently maintain your Notion system, it becomes a graveyard of outdated notes and abandoned templates. The tool rewards discipline and punishes neglect.

## When Granola Wins

Granola wins immediately when your main problem is *capturing what happened in meetings you attend*. If you're in back-to-back calls and you can't keep notes without missing what's being said, Granola solves that specific problem. You show up, you participate, you leave — and a clean summary appears.

The follow-up workflow is where Granola really helps. After a client call or a cross-functional team sync, having a structured summary ready to send means you can follow up with precise details instead of vague recollections. For client-facing work, this is the difference between looking unprepared and looking professional.

Team sharing works well when your team lives in Slack or Notion. Granola pushes summaries to where people already work, which means they're actually seen and referenced. The friction of "I should check if there's a meeting summary somewhere" disappears.

For recurring meetings, Granola builds a searchable history automatically. Finding what was decided in last quarter's planning meeting takes seconds. You don't have to remember which doc you saved it in or which date you used in your naming convention.

## When Notion Wins

Notion wins when you need a persistent, interconnected knowledge base that your team contributes to over time. If you're building a wiki, maintaining project documentation, tracking OKRs, or managing any kind of database-driven workflow, Notion is the right tool.

The flexibility is Notion's actual value proposition. A meeting note in Notion can be linked to a product spec, a user research document, and a Q4 goal simultaneously. That kind of cross-referencing is what Notion does well, and it's not something Granola tries to do.

For ongoing projects with evolving documentation, Notion's revision history, comment threads, and relational databases give you tools Granola doesn't have and isn't trying to provide.

## The Honest Take

If I had to choose only one tool for meeting notes specifically, Granola wins. The automatic capture and structured output are purpose-built for exactly this problem. Notion can hold meeting notes, but you have to do all the work of capturing and structuring them yourself.

That said, the better setup for most teams is using both. Granola handles the capture and initial structuring. Notion holds the ongoing project context that meetings connect to. Granola's Notion integration makes this actually work in practice rather than requiring manual effort.
