Court reporters are skilled professionals who have spent years mastering stenography—the ability to capture testimony at 225+ words per minute using a specialized machine. ## What Court Reporters Actually Do Beyond Stenography

The professional scope of a certified court reporter extends well beyond realtime transcription. In a typical week, a freelance reporter handles:

**Deposition preparation**: Reviewing notices, subpoenaed witness lists, document requests, and any prior testimony or related cases that might provide context.

**Post-deposition transcript annotation**: Marking speaker identifications, document references, and attorney objections in the transcript file for the court reporting firm or freelance operation.

**Exhibit management**: Tracking and indexing all exhibits referenced in testimony, noting when they're marked, offered, admitted, or refused, and maintaining a clear exhibit log.

**Summary preparation**: Drafting deposition summaries for attorneys—narrative synopses of key testimony for use in depositions, motions, or trial preparation.

**Correspondence**: Communicating with attorneys about scheduling, transcript delivery, billing questions, and coordination on complex cases.

**Scheduling and case management**: Maintaining calendars for deposition dates, hearing settings, and delivery deadlines across multiple ongoing cases.

**Billing and invoicing**: Tracking time for each appearance, preparing itemized invoices, following up on payment.

Each of these tasks involves documentation—often extensive documentation. And unlike the realtime stenography work, which has dedicated tooling and workflow, these tasks are often handled by typing or hand-writing. For reporters running high-volume practices, the administrative overhead can become a genuine bottleneck.

## Post-Hearing Transcript Annotation Via Voice

After a long hearing or deposition, the annotation and cleanup work is where reporters often spend their evening. Speaker changes need to be identified and tagged. Attorney objections need to be noted with proper legal formatting ("Objection" on its own line, objection grounds, and the ruling if given). Document references need markers.-read: and read: designations need verification.

This work is tedious but necessary for producing a clean, usable transcript. With WisprFlow, the workflow shifts: instead of typing notes about what needs to be annotated, reporters can dictate the annotation notes in real time as they review.

"First sustained objection was foundation. Second was hearsay, also sustained. Third was leading, overruled. Witness referred to Exhibit 12 at page 47, marked as Deposition Exhibit 3. Attorney Davis requested rough draft delivery by Friday."

Speaking that annotation summary takes 20 seconds. Typing it cleanly takes 2-3 minutes. For reporters handling multiple depositions per week, that's hours recovered over a month.

The dictation becomes a checklist and documentation record simultaneously. It can be reviewed before final transcript production to ensure nothing was missed, and it provides a record of what happened in the proceeding if questions arise later.

For reporters who prepare summaries frequently, the time savings are substantial. A summary that would take 45-60 minutes to type can be dictated in 15-20 minutes with a first draft that's 80% of the way to final form.

## Legal Exhibit Description and Indexing Via Voice

Exhibit management during depositions involves real-time tracking of every document referenced, marked, offered, and admitted or refused. The reporter maintains the official exhibit log, which becomes part of the transcript record.

Dictating exhibit notes via WisprFlow during the deposition (when there's a break, or immediately after) is faster than typing and captures more detail:

"Deposition Exhibit A-1: email chain dated January 15 through February 3, 2024. Subject line redacted by agreement of counsel. Marked for identification by plaintiff's counsel. Referenced at page 34, lines 5-12. Not yet offered."

The exhibit log dictation can be converted to proper format in a quick review pass. The key advantage is that the full context of how each exhibit was used is captured, which becomes important if attorneys dispute exhibit handling later.

For complex multiparty cases with 20, 30, or more exhibits, the indexing work is significant. Voice dictation makes it fast enough that reporters actually do it completely instead of doing abbreviated notes they have to reconstruct later.

## Errata Sheet Preparation Via Voice

Under Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 30(e), deponents have the right to review and sign their deposition transcripts and may make changes in form or substance, accompanied by a statement of reasons. The reporter prepares the errata sheet—listing each change requested, the location in the transcript, the original text, the proposed change, and the reasons given.

Preparing an errata sheet requires capturing changes clearly enough that they can be applied precisely to the transcript. The typical workflow involves receiving written change requests from the attorney's office and then formatting and indexing them into the proper errata sheet format.

Dictating a first draft of the errata sheet from the written change requests—instead of typing it directly—can speed up the production process significantly. The reporter speaks the changes in a consistent format, reviews the transcription, and then applies the changes to the official transcript.

"Erratum one: page 45, line 3. Original: 'I don't recall.' Change: 'I did not recall at that time.' Reason: to clarify witness's meaning regarding memory at time of prior testimony."

The structured dictation format translates directly into the errata sheet format after a quick review.
