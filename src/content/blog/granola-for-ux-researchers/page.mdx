# Granola for UX Researchers: Meeting Intelligence That Doesn't Get in the Way

UX research is a discipline built on paying attention. You run a user interview and the participant tells you something unexpected—a detail about how they actually use a feature that contradicts your hypothesis, a pain point that nobody on the product team had surfaced, a workflow that makes sense to them but would never occur to you. The insight is in the room, but only if you're actually listening.

The problem is that you're also trying to take notes. You're watching for micro-expressions while simultaneously writing down what they're saying, you're probing with follow-up questions while also capturing verbatim quotes, you're managing the interview flow while also documenting the session so you can synthesize it later. The documentation burden pulls you out of the moment exactly when the moment matters most.

Granola solves this for UX researchers the same way it solves it for anyone in a meeting-intensive role—but the specific ways it shows up in research workflows are worth spelling out, because they're substantial.

## The UX Research Documentation Stack

Before getting into how Granola helps, it's worth being specific about the actual documentation burden in a research practice.

A typical user interview runs 45-60 minutes. In that hour, you're conducting the interview—probing, observing, following threads—and also trying to capture enough of what's being said to be useful in synthesis. If you're also running a usability test, you're additionally observing behavior and trying to document what happens at each step. After the session, you need to write up findings, extract themes, pull out notable quotes for stakeholder presentations, and update your research repository.

A research cycle for a single study might involve 8-12 interviews, plus synthesis sessions, plus stakeholder readouts, plus cross-functional design critique meetings. The documentation across all of that is enormous. And unlike meeting notes for project management, research documentation has to capture nuance—the exact phrasing a user used, the emotional valence of their reaction, the specific context that made a workflow painful for them but intuitive for someone else.

Traditional approaches to this problem include: recordings with transcription (expensive, slow, compliance issues in some contexts), note-takers or co-observers (resource-intensive, sometimes changes participant behavior), typed notes that sacrifice either speed or depth (you're always choosing between capturing quantity and capturing quality). None of these fully solve the problem of being present in the moment while also capturing what's happening.

## Capturing User Interviews Without Getting in the Way

Granola runs on your Mac in the background during interviews. When you join a Zoom call with a research participant, Granola is listening. It captures the full conversation, transcribes it, and generates structured notes.

What makes this useful for research specifically is that it captures verbatim. When a participant says "I always end up just going back to the search bar because I can't figure out where anything is," Granola has that exact quote. When they demonstrate a workaround they developed and explain why they built it that way, Granola has the recording and transcript.

This matters for synthesis in ways that summary notes don't fully capture. The specific language participants use often encodes their mental model in ways that summary notes miss. When you're building an affinity map and you have access to exact quotes, you can often extract themes from participants' own phrasing rather than imposing your team's vocabulary on their experiences. The synthesis is richer when you have the raw material.

For moderated usability tests, Granola's value compounds. You're watching what users do while they're doing it, and Granola captures what they say about it. The combination of behavioral observation and verbal protocol is the gold standard in usability research, and having both captured automatically means you can focus on the observation while the verbal data is being preserved.

## Synthesis Session Documentation

Synthesis is where research insights become actionable. Affinity mapping, journey mapping, theme extraction, pattern identification—the work that turns raw interview data into something the product team can use.

The synthesis session itself is a meeting, and Granola captures it. If you're doing collaborative synthesis with a research partner—sorting through notes together, debating interpretations, building out an affinity diagram—Granola captures that conversation. When you say "I think this cluster of feedback is really about trust, not usability," that's captured. When you disagree about how to interpret a participant's response and you work through it, that reasoning is preserved.

This documentation has value for the synthesis itself. Research partners can review Granola notes from the synthesis session to refresh context before continuing. The notes serve as a record of how themes emerged and why certain interpretations were chosen over others—which is useful when you're presenting findings and stakeholders push back on your conclusions.

For research teams that run multiple studies in parallel, synthesis session documentation prevents context loss between studies. When you're three weeks into Study B and someone asks about a methodological decision you made in Study A's synthesis, you can pull up the Granola notes from that synthesis session and explain the reasoning, rather than reconstructing it from memory.

## Building the Research Repository

Every research team should have a living research repository—a searchable body of past findings that informs current product decisions and prevents redundant research. In practice, these repositories are often under-maintained because documentation is time-consuming and always seems lower-priority than the next study.

Granola makes the documentation step lower-friction. After each interview, you have a full transcript and structured notes. Converting those into research repository entries becomes a 10-minute review and tagging task rather than a 45-minute reconstruction from shorthand notes. The barrier to keeping the repository current drops significantly.

A well-maintained research repository also enables meta-analysis—what patterns show up across multiple studies? If you've run three rounds of research on the onboarding flow and participants in each round have mentioned the same friction point, that's a stronger signal than any single study. Having Granola transcripts across all three rounds makes this kind of cross-study synthesis practical.

For research ops and methodology documentation, Granola notes from team retrospectives and methodology discussions also feed the repository. When you refine your interview guide based on lessons learned, documenting that evolution in the repository helps future team members understand why the current guide looks the way it does.

## Stakeholder Presentation Prep from Meeting Notes

Research findings presentations are a specific genre of meeting with their own challenges. You're trying to convey the richness of user experiences to stakeholders who weren't in the room, using slides that inevitably flatten that richness into bullet points.

Granola gives you more to work with. When you're preparing for a stakeholder readout, you have full transcripts and verbatim quotes from every session. The presentation doesn't have to rely on your summary of what participants said—you can show them the actual language participants used. The verbatim quote that made you pause during the interview can make the same impact on the stakeholders.

A practical workflow: pull your most impactful quotes from Granola transcripts before the presentation. Identify the 3-5 moments where participants' reactions were strongest—surprise, frustration, delight, confusion. Those moments, with exact quotes, are the hooks that make research findings memorable. With Granola, finding them is a matter of searching your transcripts rather than reconstructing them from notes.

For stakeholders who want the full picture rather than summaries, Granola notes from each session can be shared directly (with participant consent and appropriate anonymization). This is useful for stakeholders who want to go deeper and for creating a culture of research consumption rather than just research output.

## Cross-Functional Design Critique Meetings

Design critiques involving research often surface tensions between what users have told you and what designers or product managers believe users want. These conversations can be productive or frustrating, and the difference often comes down to whether the discussion is grounded in evidence.

Granola captures design critique meetings with enough fidelity that you can reference specific findings when questions arise. When a designer says "users probably just need better microcopy," you can pull up the Granola transcript from your last round of research and show them what users actually said about the current microcopy—not your summary of it, but the raw participant language.

This also helps with the documentation of design decisions. When a design choice is made in a critique meeting based on a research finding, having that connection captured means the research repository can link findings to design decisions. Over time, this creates a record of how research influenced the product—which is useful for demonstrating research impact and for understanding why the product looks the way it does.

For design critiques that involve multiple stakeholders who weren't present for the original research, Granola notes give the research team a complete record of what was discussed, what concerns were raised, and how decisions were made. This prevents the "but why was this chosen?" conversation six months later when someone is confused about a design choice.

## The ROI for Research Practices

The return on investment for AI meeting notes in a research context shows up in a few different ways.

**Time recaptured from documentation.** If your current workflow involves 20-30 minutes of post-interview note-writing per session, and Granola reduces that to 5-10 minutes of review and tagging, the math is straightforward. For an 8-interview study, that's 2-3 hours of recaptured time. Across a quarter of research work, it can easily be 15-20 hours. That time goes to more research, better synthesis, or simply not working evenings to keep up.

**Richer research data.** When you're not split between listening and note-taking, you catch more. Your follow-up questions are sharper because you're not thinking about what you just missed. Your observations are more complete because you're watching rather than typing. The quality of your research improves because you're fully present in the sessions that produce your data.

**Faster synthesis.** Having full transcripts and structured notes from sessions makes synthesis faster and more thorough. You can extract themes from actual quotes rather than reconstructed summaries. Your affinity diagrams are built from real language rather than your interpretation of it. The synthesis step that typically takes days compresses because the raw material is already organized.

**Better stakeholder communication.** When you can show stakeholders verbatim quotes and exact participant language, your presentations land differently. You're not asking them to trust your summary of user feedback—you're showing them the feedback directly. This builds confidence in research findings and increases the odds that insights actually influence product decisions.

## Practical Setup for UX Research Teams

Getting started with Granola in a research context takes about 15 minutes and a few decisions.

**Set up consent protocols for recording.** Granola captures audio and generates transcripts. If you're working with participants who have signed consent forms, make sure those forms explicitly cover recording for research documentation purposes. Most IRB-approved research consent forms do cover this, but it's worth verifying.

**Create session templates for different research types.** Granola lets you set context for meetings, and a brief template for user interviews versus usability tests versus stakeholder meetings will produce more useful output. A template that reminds the AI to extract quotes, pain points, and unexpected moments will surface the most useful research content.

**Tag and organize notes by study.** Using a consistent naming convention for Granola notes—Study name, participant ID or session number, date—makes the research repository organization straightforward. Searchable, organized notes are a research repository waiting to be built.

**Review and annotate rather than starting from scratch.** The workflow with Granola isn't "dictate and forget"—it's "capture and refine." After each session, review the Granola notes, correct anything that needs correction, and tag key moments. This 5-10 minute review step is where the notes become research-grade documentation.
