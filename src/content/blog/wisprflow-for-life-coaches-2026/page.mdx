## Why Typing Fails Life Coaches Specifically

Typing session notes after a coaching session requires reconstructing a verbal, emotional, dynamic conversation into a written form. This translation is inherently lossy, and it gets worse under time pressure.

Life coaching generates specific kinds of documentation challenges that typing handles poorly:

**Exact client language matters**. Coaching is built on reflecting language back to clients. When a client says "I feel trapped by my own standards" — not "stressed" or "overwhelmed" but that specific phrase — that's clinically significant. It's the phrase you'll return to in future sessions. Typed notes tend to paraphrase. Voice notes, dictated immediately, preserve the precision.

**Emotional texture is non-verbal**. How a client said something — the hesitation before a breakthrough, the energy shift when they stopped defending and started accepting — doesn't survive typed documentation well. Voice notes capture your real-time observation: "Client's voice changed when she talked about her mother. Less defensive. First time she used past tense about that relationship."

**Coaching is iterative over long timeframes**. You're tracking a client across months or years. Session notes from month three need to connect to themes you first noticed in month one. Detailed, accurate documentation compounds over time. Vague impressions don't.
