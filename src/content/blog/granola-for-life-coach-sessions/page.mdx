# Granola for Life Coaches: Session Documentation That Captures Client Breakthroughs

The moment a client says something that shifts everything for them — the insight they arrived at, the connection they made, the exact language they used — you're writing notes instead of being present. You're hearing the words and simultaneously trying to figure out how to abbreviate them in your notebook. The transcription quality you provide yourself during a breakthrough moment is terrible by design, because you can't fully listen and take full notes at the same time.

Granola puts the notebook down for you. The session is transcribed automatically. You stay fully present with your client and review the complete record afterward, including the exact words that matter most.

## The Documentation Gap in Coaching

Life coaches need documentation for three distinct purposes, and the current approach fails all three:

**Progress tracking across sessions.** A client has been working on boundary-setting for four months. In session one, they described the problem in one way. By session eight, their language has shifted — they've internalized a different framing of their situation. That linguistic shift *is* the progress. But your notes from session one are a paraphrase. The exact words from session one are gone. You can't compare the client's actual language then versus now.

**Follow-up personalization.** The client mentioned last session that their mother called and it triggered the old pattern. This session, you reference it — but you're recalling your note, not the client's specific words. The note said "triggered by mom call." The client actually said "when my mom calls, I hear her voice in my head saying I'm not trying hard enough, and then I freeze for the rest of the day." That specific language is the coaching material. "Triggered by mom call" is a dead note.

**Pattern identification.** Certain phrases recur across sessions. A client keeps saying "I should be able to handle this" — or "I'm afraid of disappointing everyone" — or "I feel guilty when I choose myself." Those recurring phrases are the key to the work. They indicate the stories the client is still running. But when the phrases get compressed to notes at the moment of utterance, the recurrence pattern is invisible.

## How Granola Works in Coaching Sessions

For coaches who do virtual sessions — by video or phone — Granola runs as a Mac app and transcribes automatically when it detects the call.

After the session, you get a full transcript and an AI-generated summary. The summary surfaces key discussion points, any commitments or goals that came up, and the main themes of the conversation.

The practical workflow:

**During the session:** You're fully present. You're listening deeply. You're asking the next question from your intuition, not from a note you're scribbling. The client is talking and you're hearing every word without filtering for what to write down.

**After the session:** You review the transcript and summary. You highlight the breakthrough moments — the client's exact words where they cracked something open. You note the shifts in language. You flag anything to follow up on next time. You spend five to ten minutes doing this instead of the current fifteen to twenty minutes of trying to reconstruct what happened.

**Before the next session:** You pull up your review from the previous session. You have the client's exact language from where they were. You can start from that anchor: "Last time you said that you've been running on 'I should be able to handle this.' What's happened since then?" That's a better opening than "Last session you mentioned some anxiety around your work situation."

## Capturing Breakthrough Language

Breakthrough moments in coaching are language events. The client finds words for something they've been feeling. Those specific words — not your paraphrase of them — are what carry the power.

"I realized I've been punishing myself for something that wasn't my fault."

"I can hear myself choosing their comfort over my health, and I can see it now."

"I don't actually believe I'm not good enough. I believe my mother thinks I'm not good enough, and I've been living as if that's true."

Each of those sentences, if you capture it verbatim, becomes a touchstone. The client can recognize themselves in it. You can reference it in future sessions without misquoting. The exact language has therapeutic power that paraphrases don't.

Granola captures the breakthrough language verbatim. You go back to the transcript and find the moment. You use it in the next session. The client feels seen because you remembered not just the gist of what they said, but what they actually said.

## Session-to-Session Continuity

The arc of a coaching engagement unfolds across months. Each session builds on what came before. The quality of that continuity depends entirely on how well the coach tracks where the client was and where they're moving.

With Granola, session-to-session continuity improves in concrete ways:

**You open the next session from where the client actually was, not where you think they were.** "Last time you described feeling stuck between two choices. You said, 'Either way I lose something I love.' Let's check in on that feeling — has it shifted?" That opening shows the client you were listening carefully. It also gives the client an anchor to measure their own movement from.

**You notice the language shifts that indicate progress.** The client who was saying "I can't" three months ago and is now saying "I'm choosing not to" has made enormous progress. If your notes say "still struggling with boundaries" and don't capture the language, you might miss the shift. The transcript catches it.

**You notice the patterns that indicate where the real work is.** The recurring phrases, the specific scenarios that always come up, the emotional reactions that repeat — these patterns are the coaching material. Granola transcripts let you search across sessions for specific themes and find every instance.

## Client Privacy and Informed Consent

Coaching sessions contain deeply personal information. Before running Granola on client calls, coaches need informed consent: the client should know the session is being transcribed, what the transcript will be used for, and how it will be stored.

Practical approach: inform the client during the intake session that you use an AI transcription tool to capture session notes, that the transcription helps you track their progress more accurately, and that you handle the transcripts with the same confidentiality as your other session notes. Most clients will appreciate the commitment to accuracy.

Some coaches let clients know at the start of each session: "I'll be recording this for my notes — is that okay?" This brief consent reminder is good practice regardless of transcription tool.

Granola stores transcripts locally on the coach's machine. You control the data. For coaches with specific data handling requirements — HIPAA-aligned practices, professional ethics standards around record retention — check that Granola's storage model meets your obligations before adopting it.

## Scaling Across Your Practice

The aggregate value of Granola across a full coaching practice is substantial.

When you're seeing 20 to 30 clients per week, session notes become a documentation burden. The current model: write notes after every session, maintain a running summary of each client's progress, review your notes before the next session, update the summary. This takes meaningful time and the quality degrades under volume pressure.

With Granola, each session generates its own record automatically. Your post-session work is reviewing and annotating, not writing from scratch. The review takes five to ten minutes instead of the current fifteen to twenty minutes of reconstruction. Across a full client load, that's hours per week recovered.

The AI-generated summaries also surface themes you might miss across your full caseload. If the summary of your Tuesday session mentions "fear of failure" and the summary of your Thursday session mentions "fear of rejection" — these are related themes you might connect in your own supervision or peer consultation work.

For coaches who do supervision or are pursuing credentials that require session documentation, Granola transcripts provide the level of detail those processes need without requiring coaches to manually maintain comprehensive notes under the time pressure of a full practice.

## What Changes in Your Practice

The shift from manual notes to Granola transcription changes two things about the session itself:

**First, you're more present.** When you know everything is being captured, you stop filtering for note-worthy moments during the conversation. You listen more completely. Your responses come from full presence instead of partial presence plus note-tracking. Clients notice this, even if they can't articulate why the session feels different.

**Second, your session openings improve.** Instead of starting from a summary of the last session — "So last time we talked about your work relationship" — you start from specifics. "Last time you said something that stuck with me. You said, 'I've been showing up as half a person at work.' What does it feel like to hear that back?" Specific openings from the client's own language create faster depth in the next session.

Both changes improve outcomes. The first improves the quality of the session in real time. The second improves the continuity and depth of the coaching arc.
