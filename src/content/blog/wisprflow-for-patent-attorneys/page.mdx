Patent prosecution is one of the most writing-intensive legal practices in existence. A single patent application might require 30-50 pages of dense technical claims, descriptions, and drawings references. An office action response can run another 20-40 pages of arguments, claim amendments, and remarks. Attorneys who type all of this are spending hours on keyboarding that could go toward actual legal analysis.

WisprFlow converts speech to text at 179 words per minute — roughly three times the average typing speed. For patent attorneys, that speed difference directly translates to time recovered for thinking, not transcribing.

## The Specific Documents That Eat Your Time

Patent prosecution involves a predictable set of documents that attorneys draft over and over: independent claims, dependent claims, specification sections, office action responses, appeals briefs, and client prosecution letters. Each has its own structural conventions, but all of them require large volumes of precise language.

Independent claims are particularly painful to type. A well-drafted independent claim for a software patent might read: "A computer-implemented method comprising: receiving, by a processor, a first data structure comprising a plurality of user-defined parameters; executing, in response to receiving the first data structure, a validation routine configured to compare each of the plurality of user-defined parameters against a stored schema." 

Typing that while thinking through claim scope is cognitively expensive. The mechanics of typing interrupt the legal reasoning. When you dictate instead, you can speak the claim language continuously, adjusting phrasing as you go, without the stop-and-start rhythm that typing imposes.

## Dictating Claim Structures

Nested claim structures are where dictation pays off most. Dependent claims that reference earlier claims, method claims that parallel apparatus claims, continuation applications where you're adapting claim sets from parent applications — all of this involves a lot of repetitive structural language.

With WisprFlow, you speak into any text field on your Mac, and the text appears. That means you can dictate directly into your patent drafting software, into Word, into a Google Doc, or into your firm's docketing system. There's no intermediate step of recording to a separate file and sending it to a transcription service.

A practical workflow: open your claim draft, position your cursor at the start of the first independent claim, activate WisprFlow, and speak the entire claim in one pass. Then dictate "dependent claim two, the method of claim one wherein..." and continue through the claim set. You can speak preamble language, transition phrases like "comprising" and "consisting essentially of," and element language all in sequence.

For the specification, dictate your detailed description section by section. Patent specifications follow a recognizable structure — brief description of drawings, detailed description of preferred embodiments, alternative embodiments. You can speak through each section while keeping your figures visible on a second monitor, narrating what each figure illustrates.

## Office Action Responses

Office action responses require a different kind of writing than initial applications. You're making arguments, quoting claims, quoting examiner rejections, distinguishing prior art. The document is dialogic — you're responding to specific points raised by the examiner.

Dictating responses works well because the argumentative structure maps naturally to speech. You can speak: "The examiner has rejected claims one through five under 35 U.S.C. 103 over Smith in view of Jones. Applicant respectfully traverses this rejection for the following reasons." Then you speak your arguments directly.

The remarks section of an office action response is where dictation creates the most time savings. A thorough remarks section addressing multiple rejections might run 10-15 pages. Speaking arguments rather than typing them lets you maintain the thread of your reasoning without the mechanical overhead of keyboarding.

## Appeals Briefs and PTAB Practice

Appeals briefs before the Patent Trial and Appeal Board require formal argument structure: statement of facts, argument sections, conclusion. These documents average 30-50 pages and require sustained, coherent writing.

Attorneys who dictate appeals briefs report that speaking forces a clarity of argument structure that typing doesn't. When you're speaking aloud, vague reasoning is more apparent — you can hear when your argument doesn't follow. That's a subtle benefit beyond pure speed: dictation as a draft review mechanism.

For inter partes review petitions and responses, the same principle applies. These documents require detailed claim construction arguments, claim charts, and technical analysis. Dictating the argument sections while reviewing prior art references on screen is faster than typing while switching between windows.

## Client Prosecution Letters

Client letters explaining office actions, reporting allowances, and advising on continuation strategy are written in plain language compared to prosecution documents, but they still take time. Attorneys often delay these letters because they're one more writing task on top of drafting and responding.

Dictating client letters takes minutes rather than an hour. You can explain a 103 rejection, walk through the proposed response strategy, and outline costs in a five-minute dictation session. The letter is ready for review and signature without an extended typing session.

## Getting the Terminology Right

Patent attorneys who try voice-to-text tools often abandon them because legal and technical terminology gets mangled. Section numbers, claim phrases, Latin legal terms, and technical vocabulary all require accurate transcription.

WisprFlow handles specialized vocabulary well because it uses context to improve transcription accuracy. Patent-specific terms like "wherein," "configured to," "operably coupled," "non-transitory computer-readable medium," and standard claim transition phrases transcribe accurately once the model has context from your document. Legal citations like "35 U.S.C. 102" and "37 CFR 1.111" transcribe cleanly.

If a term doesn't transcribe correctly, you can pause, correct it by voice or keyboard, and continue. The workflow is not all-or-nothing — you can dictate large sections and make targeted edits rather than dictating every character.
