## What Apple Dictation Actually Does Well

Apple Dictation is genuinely impressive for free software. The accuracy on casual speech is surprisingly high. I dictated emails, short messages, and general notes without major issues. The system-wide availability means you can use it anywhere — every text field, every app, no setup required.

The continuous dictation mode works well for longer pieces. You can speak naturally and the results are legible. For someone who just wants to capture thoughts without touching the keyboard, Apple Dictation handles the basics.

It's also completely invisible. No extra window, no separate app to launch, no subscription to manage. You hit the shortcut, you talk, you move on.

## Where Apple Dictation Breaks Down

The problems start when you need it to understand what you're actually doing.

**Code is a foreign language.** When I said "const fetch user from database", Apple Dictation transcribed it as "const fetch user from database" — which looks correct until you realize it didn't understand the code context. It can't distinguish between a variable declaration and a sentence. It doesn't know that "from" is a keyword in some contexts and filler in others. The output needs manual correction before it compiles.

**No screen awareness.** Apple Dictation has no concept of what's on your screen. It can't see your open files, your current project, or your recent work. It treats every dictation session in isolation. For coding, this matters — you need a tool that understands the context you're working in.

**No intelligent formatting.** Spoken language doesn't map cleanly to code. When I say "if error not nil return early", I expect the tool to understand that I want a guard clause with proper syntax. Apple Dictation just gives me the words. Someone still has to turn those words into working code.

**No VS Code integration.** This is the practical killer. Apple Dictation doesn't know about your editor, your cursor position, or your project structure. You dictate somewhere, then you copy and paste. That's not voice coding — that's voice typing into a void.

## The Honest Verdict

Apple Dictation is fine for casual text input. If you're dictating emails, messages, or notes without code, it's competent and free. There's no reason to pay for a dedicated tool if that's your use case. The voice recognition itself is solid — Apple has clearly invested in this. The transcription accuracy on plain English is genuinely good.

But if you're serious about voice coding, Apple Dictation doesn't cut it. The lack of code awareness, screen context, and editor integration means you're fighting the tool more than using it. You'll spend as much time correcting as you would typing. For code, the difference isn't in the speech recognition itself — it's in everything else.
