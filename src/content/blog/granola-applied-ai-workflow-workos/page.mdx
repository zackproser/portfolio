## What Granola is, in one paragraph

Granola is a Mac app that records the audio on your machine — your microphone and the system output, both — without joining the call as a participant. There is no bot in the meeting. There is no notification to the other side. After the call ends, Granola produces a structured summary based on a template you pick, with the full transcript one click away. It connects to your calendar and prompts you to start capture a minute before each meeting. iOS and Windows versions exist. The one on my laptop is the one I live in.

That is all it does. Most of what makes it good is what it does *not* do.

## The four meeting shapes that drive my week

### 1. Exec syncs

These are thirty-minute cadences with peers and VPs. The shape is dense: status across multiple initiatives, decisions queued for input, surfaces where I need air cover, places I need to pull on a thread someone else owns. The cost of missing a commitment in an exec sync is high — it shows up as the same conversation, repeated, two weeks later, with worse vibes.

Granola is excellent at the structural parts. Decisions land in the summary cleanly. Owner assignments come through with names attached. Follow-ups read like they were written by someone who was actually in the room — which, in a way, they were.

The part I still do by hand is org-chart context. Granola can&apos;t infer that the person committing to a thing is the right owner versus the wrong owner. I&apos;ll skim the summary, move a couple of items to different names, and ship it to the relevant Slack channel. Two minutes of cleanup on a thirty-minute meeting.

### 2. 1:1s

A different shape entirely. Less about decisions, more about context: how someone is doing, what they&apos;re stuck on, what they&apos;re going to want my air cover for in the next two weeks. The notes I want from a 1:1 are not the same notes I want from an exec sync.

I keep notes minimal in the moment — almost zero — and let Granola do the structural lifting. After the call, I separate the output into two pieces: *what we decided* (which I&apos;ll reference next time) and *what we discussed* (which is for me, often archived after a read-through). The "decided" half lives in my prep doc for the next 1:1. The "discussed" half lives in my weekly review.

I do not share the raw transcript back with the person I just spoke to. The summary is for me. What gets surfaced back to them — in Slack, in a follow-up email, in the next 1:1 — is something I wrote, informed by the record. The record is private. The reflection is the thing I share.

### 3. Customer calls

The compliance and consent shape. The local-audio architecture is what makes this work — there is no third-party bot joining, which means no third-party data path to disclose. That changes what I can responsibly capture in calls with customers who haven&apos;t signed something specific about a vendor in the loop.

I still tell people. The point is not to capture covertly; the point is that the *technical* footprint is small enough that telling them lands as "I&apos;m taking notes" rather than "I&apos;ve added a recording bot to our meeting." People understand the first sentence. The second sentence has historically gotten me asked to turn things off.

The post-call workflow is straightforward: read the summary, lift the top items into a Slack thread for the account team, and update the CRM. Five minutes for a thirty-minute call, on a good day.

### 4. Technical design reviews

This is where Granola is weakest, and the place I&apos;m most willing to say it out loud.

Architecture conversations don&apos;t reduce well into bullet summaries. The shape of the thing — *we considered X, we ruled it out for these failure modes, we landed on Y because of these constraints* — is exactly the kind of structured reasoning that an LLM summarizer flattens. You end up with a list of statements that are individually true and collectively useless, because the *order* and *because* are gone.

What I do instead: Granola captures, like always, but I write the Architecture Decision Record by hand using the transcript as source material. The transcript is good — I can search for the moment someone said "the consistency model breaks if we do that" and pull the surrounding two minutes. The summary is the wrong granularity for an ADR. The transcript is exactly the right granularity.

Granola is the input; the document is mine.

## What lives in the meeting itself

There&apos;s a thing that doesn&apos;t fit cleanly under a meeting shape because it&apos;s not about a particular call — it&apos;s about *being* in any call.

I lose the thread mid-meeting. Especially at work, where I&apos;m juggling six projects, four codebases, and twelve open Linear tickets across two teams, the cognitive load of holding the conversation plus the rest of the day in working memory is real. I miss things. Used to be: smile, nod, hope it shakes out by the time I&apos;m supposed to weigh in.

Granola has a live query feature — ask it, in-call, *what was just said*, or *clarify that last phrase I missed*, and it answers. Not from the eventual summary; from the running transcript, live, while the meeting is still happening.

The cost of momentary distraction drops from "I just missed something I&apos;ll need in twenty minutes" to "I&apos;ll re-read the last sixty seconds and rejoin." That second cost is fine. The first cost is what made meetings feel expensive.

## My prompt pack

Six templates I actually use. Granola lets you set a default template per meeting type or override per-meeting. These are the ones I&apos;ve refined over a year:

1. **Exec sync** — three sections: *decisions taken*, *commitments by owner*, *open threads to chase*. No prose. Action items get bolded. Anything tagged "open" gets carried to the next sync if it isn&apos;t closed before.
2. **1:1 with a report** — two sections: *what we discussed* and *what was committed*. The second section is what I bring to the next 1:1. The first section gets archived.
3. **1:1 with my manager** — same structure as the report 1:1, plus a third section: *what I asked for* (air cover, headcount, context I need from elsewhere in the org). That third section is the most useful one for me as I track what I&apos;m repeatedly asking for.
4. **Customer call** — *what they care about*, *what they pushed back on*, *what we owe them*, *what the account team should know*. The last bucket goes to Slack within the hour.
5. **Design review** — short summary plus a *decisions to capture in the ADR* block. The full transcript is the actual source for the doc.
6. **Catch-up with a friend** — yes, I run it for these. Single section: *things to follow up on next time*. That is the whole template. It has made me a meaningfully better friend.

The last one is the one most people are surprised by. I&apos;m the most surprised by how much I depend on it.

## Where it breaks down

The honest section.

**Long technical whiteboarding.** When the conversation depends on a diagram that&apos;s being drawn, the transcript loses the shape. The references to "this box" and "that arrow" produce a summary that&apos;s about as useful as nothing.

**Multi-speaker debates without clear turn-taking.** Three or four engineers all making the same point at the same time, with disagreement on the second point that overlaps with agreement on the third — Granola gets the words but loses who-said-what enough that I can&apos;t reconstruct the negotiation. I treat these meetings as "transcript only, no summary."

**Calls in languages other than English.** I&apos;ve had a handful of consulting calls in Spanish and the summary quality drops sharply. Transcript is OK. Summary is generic. I assume this will close over time.

**Meetings where the substantive content was a screen share.** Granola hears what was said about the demo. It does not see the demo. For "did the click-through feel right" conversations, the summary is structurally insufficient.

None of these are deal-breakers. They&apos;re shapes where I treat Granola as "transcript-only" and write the artifact myself.

## How to try it without losing the week

If you&apos;re going to install Granola today, don&apos;t do what I did and try to capture everything from day one. The week-one play that actually works:

1. **Pick one meeting type to start with.** I&apos;d say 1:1s. They&apos;re short, low-stakes, and the structure of the output is easy to evaluate.
2. **Use the default template.** Don&apos;t build your own prompt pack yet. You don&apos;t know what you want from the output until you&apos;ve seen a week of defaults.
3. **Read every summary the day it lands.** Build an opinion. If something is consistently missing or wrong, that&apos;s the shape of your eventual custom template.
4. **Expand after a week.** Add a second meeting type. Add a third. Build your prompt pack one template at a time, anchored to a meeting shape you actually attend.
5. **Tell people.** The local-audio architecture lets you be silent. That doesn&apos;t mean you should be. "I&apos;m taking notes with an AI tool" is one sentence. It costs nothing. It also closes the consent loop and you stop having to think about it.

The first week of running Granola is the only week where it&apos;s a tax. By week two you stop noticing it&apos;s there, except for the part where you start noticing how much less you&apos;re carrying.
